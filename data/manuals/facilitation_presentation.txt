3:10pm Let’s start with: Why do we have moderators during every Citizens’
Assembly? [Wait for responses, take notes as we go along, then reveal a slide with an overview from HealthDemocracy and touch on anything that hasn’t been raised yet]:
• Creating a welcoming & warm environment.
• Give grace and care for Delegates as human beings first and
foremost
• Being cognizant of people’s personal relationships with
climate catastrophes and it’s very personal dangers and varied impacts on different regions or delegates call home – these can be tough or even traumatic to talk about
• Timekeeping – keeping groups moving through the agenda efficiently.
• Making on-the-spot decisions to balance when groups need to be pushed forward vs. when to slow down and take more time with an activity.
• Clarifying purpose
• Reminding groups of higher-level goals, outcomes, and
process, arc.
• Monitoring energy levels – Tuning in to Delegates’ overall
engagement and attentiveness. Taking action to meet physical needs if necessary (e.g., taking a bio break, stretching)
• Balancing levels of participation – Noting who is speaking most/least and taking steps to encourage the sharing of air time.
• Be a guide – All your work should aim to help a group do its best thinking. Take steps to take yourself out of the conversation, encouraging Delegates to talk to each other and not to/through you.
• Normalize silence!
• Silence can be useful for people thinking through their
responses. But also: people in the group will fill that silence!
• Encourage understanding – Bring group’s attention to
misunderstandings between Panelists and give time to working through them.
• But this doesn’t mean you encourage agreement. In fact, understanding is reached by putting a spotlight on points of disagreement until all sides understand why they disagree
• Interrupting problematic behaviors - interruptions, debating, personal slights, especially microaggressions and unintentional harm which is far more likely than intentional insults
PART II What are our moderators NOT supposed to do?
• Don’t be a decision maker
• This is a new paradigm of democracy..
• Reminder: the Delegates are the ones who are reflective of the
university, not us. They’re the ones with the most legitimacy.
• Imagine – even though they’re only recommendations – that we are support staff to a pseudo-legislative body. As much as possible, we defer to them.
• Don’t lead or direct. And becoming more so with each new project.
• Designing for as much transfer of power as possible – away
from staff and to Delegates.
• Delegates will be setting their own agenda (from a content
perspective).
• As usual, Delegates will be writing and editing all of their own
content.
• Language: “Delegates” or “Assembly members.” Or really
anything but “participants.”
• So, our role is as advisors or moderators, not facilitators.
• We are (at most) the tour manager of the orchestra, not the conductor or artistic director. Or a bit like the city staff sitting at the side of the City Council dias.
• The less we’re involved, the better.
• Non-directive. Not looking for solutions or agreement.
• We are not there to police rules or teach anyone anything.
We’re there as support staff to the Delegates.
• What to do: Help orient to the process, with as many
rationales as possible. ■ Delegates always deserve a “why” whenever being
told anything.
• What to do: Help reinforce an environment of mutual respect.
Primarily: ■ Sharing airtime. ■ Respect for differing individuals and views.
• Don’t use your knowledge of sustainability - that’s not your role.
• Separation between content and process (and evaluation).
• No content-related questions. If the discussion needs a little
priming, though, non-content questions are great. Here are some of our favorites:
■ What else would be important to understand? ■ What would the impact be? ■ What else? ■ Tell me more. ■ Say more about that. ■ Why is that important to you? ■ What would that accomplish for you? ■ Who hasn’t had a chance to weigh in? ■ What is your understanding of that? ■ Help us understand.
■ Can you summarize what you’ve heard? ■ What is one thing that stands out for you right now? ■ Take a minute and write down some ideas or
questions you have. ■ What hasn’t been said?
• No fact-checking.
• No intervention unless for reasons of respect (e.g., airtime),
Delegate wellbeing, or process clarification.
3:25pm Let’s go into breakout rooms for ~8-10 min and you’ll each be assigned one
of 3 problem scenarios that could come up during the assembly. Brainstorm how you might handle them to keep everyone supported without leading too much or demonstrating who you agree with in the situation! Some tools to keep at the top of your mind as you’re troubleshooting: 1. Demonstrate active listening (E.g., nodding, showing interest, summarizing, empathizing) 2. Focus on experiences, not opinion (“What’s the story behind that?”) 3. Invite people to respond to each other - remember it's a conversation 4. Point back to group norms if you get off track 5. Offer resources (E.g., don’t be their therapist or cross boundaries) 6. Model mistakes & apologies
• Scenario 1: Your group is drafting one of their reports, and two members are in disagreement about how to phrase a specific detail. One member says to another, “Just let me do this part, I learned how to write like this in my philosophy of law class and my dad’s a lawyer, so it’ll be much faster if I just do it.”
• Scenario 2: You are moderating a small group that you know includes members who identify as physically disabled. In a discussion about whether we should ban plastic straws at MIT, a delegate says, “I hate people who use plastic straws – there’s no way you’re so lazy that you can’t pick up a cup and just drink from it like a normal person.”
• Scenario 3: Your group is really easy going and everyone seems to agree that MIT should divest completely from fossil fuels by the end of the year. They want to wrap up their conversation early, because the issue is simple and they’re done. But, one person hasn’t spoken much, except to mumble, “Sure” or nod their head. How do you keep the conversation going in a productive and inclusive direction?
• Scenario 4: One delegate keeps mispronouncing another delegates’ name, even after being gently corrected by other delegates in the room. They also tend to talk over the person, cutting them off mid-sentence and then directing a question towards the moderator (e.g., “But moderator, that’s not really relevant, right?”). How do you address these awkward and disrespectful dynamics?
What moderation tools helped?
3:40pm Ok, if we’re going to be supportive without letting our biases get in the way,
let’s figure out what our biases are. Let’s open up this MURAL link so you can anonymously leave your responses under each question. I’ll give you about 90 seconds per question and then we’ll speed onto the next one, because I really want your first instinct, even if it’s just from what you hear on TV or images that pop into your mind first - this is when our biases come out most. Let’s do this practice question altogether:
- What do environmental activists look like? (What assumptions do we make about their socioeconomic status, age, or educational background?)
- You can pop in images - You can use the sticky notes to write descriptions - You can use emojis
Everyone understand this method? Ok! Let’s do 4 more questions and then reflect on what the results are looking like.
• What do MIT administrators think about student activists? How do they feel/react when getting student input or feedback?
• Why are some students NOT environmentalists or don’t care about sustainability much?
• Who deserves to be making decisions about the future of MIT? Can someone summarize our results for me? What are the prevalent biases we need to be aware of? What we can do:
- Usually, we subconsciously share more affirmation and more engagement with ideas we like or agree with – try to encourage ideas
and hear more from the people that you don’t instinctively like or agree with - also simply people that we look like!
- Deeper understanding comes from understanding the experiences behind someone’s opinions -
- That’s really interesting! Can you say more? - What’s the story behind that? - When did you start believing that? Did you used to
believe something different? Why? - Not in an interrogative way, just genuine curiosity and
wanting to hear more when you’re confused or caught off guard
- When you hear something that surprises you or goes against your biases, express gratitude & ask to hear more
- Encourage delegates to get curious and look for more information! For example, a lot of people associate environmental activism with people throwing soup on Van Gogh paintings and that’s a visceral negative association. Is there more to that story? Were those activists just weird extremists, or was there more going on in the context?
