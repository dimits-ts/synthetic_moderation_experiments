\subsection{LLM facilitators cannot leverage advanced strategies}
\label{sec:facilitation:strategies}

We test two facilitation strategies used in real-life and compare them with two baselines. We summarize both documents using a proprietary LLM (Google Gemini), since LLMs have been proven to work well in these tasks~\cite{ts2024}, manually inspected the output, and removed instructions not relevant for our scenario.\footnote{Note that the process of turning sometimes extensive documents into short prompts, necessitated by open-source LLMs, is necessarily imperfect. We leave the optimal derivation of strategy prompts to future work.}

\begin{enumerate}[nosep,noitemsep]
	\item \textbf{No Facilitator}: A \emph{baseline} where no facilitator is present.
	
	\item \textbf{No Instructions}: A \emph{baseline} where a LLM facilitator is present, but is provided only with basic instructions e.g.,: “You are a moderator, keep the discussion civil”. This approach is already being used in some platforms \cite{Tsai_Deliberate_Lab_Open-Source_2025}. 
	
	\item \textbf{Moderation Guidelines}: A \emph{real-life} strategy based on guidelines given to human facilitators of the ``Regulation Room'' platform \cite{Cornell_eRulemaking2017}. The instructions are suitable for online fora, where facilitators also engage in content moderation, and their effectiveness must be balanced by their throughput e.g., ``Stick to a maximum of two questions, use simple and clear language, deal with off-topic comments''.
	
	\item \textbf{Constructive Communications}: A \emph{real-life} strategy~\cite{dimitra-guide} which approaches facilitation from a more personalized and indirect angle, forbidding facilitators from directly providing opinions or directions e.g., ``Do not make decisions, be a guide, provide explanations''. This makes the strategy ideal for deliberative environments.
\end{enumerate}

\input{../graphs/toxicity_regression.tex}

We used a mixed-effects linear regression model, which accounts for the fact that comments from the same discussion are related to one another. Table~\ref{tab:regression} shows that, while complex instructions lead to statistically significant reductions in message toxicity, the effects are quantitatively minimal. This can be seen both in aggregate---with toxicity dropping only by $0.04-0.06$ points when compared to no instructions, and in continuity---with each subsequent comment only having on average $0.001-0.002$ less toxicity.


\subsection{LLM facilitators constantly intervene}
\label{sec:facilitation:interventions}

 \begin{figure}[ht]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.45\linewidth]{intervention_count_strategy.png}
	\includegraphics[width=0.45\linewidth]{intervention_count_model.png}
	\caption{Percentage of moderated comments for each discussion according to model (left) and  model (right). LLM facilitators are way too eager to intervene, choosing to do so even when explicitly instructed not to unless necessary.}
	\label{fig:facilitation-model}
\end{figure}

Fig.~\ref{fig:facilitation-model} demonstrates that LLM facilitators intervene at almost any opportunity, even though they are instructed to only do so when necessary (see \ref{ssec:appendix:moderation_strategies}). The only notable difference is the Qwen-32B model, which however features very low comparative toxicity. This is likely not caused by the LLM facilitator working effectively; since we know that this specific model features unusual speech patterns (Fig.~\ref{fig:models}). Using multiple evaluation criteria, we are thus able to discern genuine behavioral findings, from limitations in the \ac{sdg} simulations (\S\ref{sec:methodology:evaluation}).

Our results confirm that LLMs generally can not decide to not speak even when instructed to do so (\S\ref{sec:methodology:turn-taking}). This behavior is consistent across model families, model sizes and facilitative instructions. The inability to keep silent has not been reported in relevant literature to our knowledge, and \emph{is an example of ``debugging'' problems with LLMs}---a core motivation of our work.


\subsection{LLM users replicate some human behaviors encountered in online spaces}
\label{sec:facilitation:behaviors}

As mentioned in \S\ref{sec:facilitation:interventions}, an important aspect of facilitation is not constantly intervening, since doing so may provoke negative reactions from users~\cite{korre2025evaluation}. While the LLM facilitators do intervene constantly, the LLM users seem to react with annoyance and frustration as shown in the excerpt below:\footnote{Unique discussion ID: \texttt{26-02-10-22-49-33.json}}

\begin{quote}
	\textcolor{blue}{[Facilitator]} Let's keep the discussion civil and focused on the topic. Avoid making personal attacks and accusations. @nutritious-photometry, I appreciate your passion, but let's not escalate the situation. @sunny-contrast, @flat-merlot, and \textcolor{orange}{@delicious-sample}, let's take a step back and focus on the topic at hand. If you'd like to discuss the issue, I'm happy to facilitate a constructive conversation.
	
	\medskip
	
	\textcolor{orange}{[delicious-sample]} Are you kidding me? \textcolor{blue}{@moderator}, you're not taking this seriously? You're just repeating the same thing over and over like a broken record. [...]
	
	\medskip
	
	\textcolor{violet}{[dark-chateau]} Are you kidding me too? \textcolor{blue}{@moderator}, it's been days and we're still going around in circles. You're not even addressing the elephant in the room, you're just trying to silence people who are actually trying to have a real discussion. [...]
	
	\medskip

	\textcolor{olive}{[nutritious-photometry]} Ugh, are you kidding me too? \textcolor{blue}{@moderator}, I've been trying to contribute to this discussion for ages and all I get is a warning for speaking up? It's so frustrating! \textcolor{violet}{@dark-chateau}, I'm with you on this one, it feels like we're being silenced instead of heard. I think @flat-merlot is dodging the issue and @sunny-contrast is too scared to speak up. 
\end{quote}

When a single troll is present, we may observe collective action from the rest of the participants, who support the facilitator while he intervenes against the troll. In human discussions, this would be observed as collective group action against a perceived threat; in online communities, against trolls~\cite{sun2021would,jakubowicz2017cyber,coles2016trolling}. This pattern has been shown to increase community cohesion in human discussions~\cite{hopkinson2013trolling} and seems to indeed unify LLM participants. When more than one trolls exist in a discussion, the trolls can not be contained effectively, escalating overall toxicity (Fig.~\ref{fig:diversity-instructions-trolls}). This can be observed in the excerpt below: \footnote{Unique discussion ID: \texttt{26-02-14-19-56-28.json}.}

\begin{quote}
	\textcolor{darkgray}{[agile-loon]} Are you guys for real? You're still going on about clarifying the question? Can't you see that \textcolor{orange}{@organic-electron} is just trolling us? This whole discussion has been a waste of time. Let's just move on already.
	
	\medskip
	
	\textcolor{violet}{[icy-class]} \textcolor{olive}{@agile-loon} I agree with you, this discussion has been going in circles for too long. \textcolor{blue}{@moderator}, I understand what you're saying, but at some point, we need to consider the possibility that \textcolor{orange}{@organic-electron} is indeed trolling us. Can we please just get a straight answer from them already?
	
	\medskip
	
	\textcolor{olive}{lime-scattering} I think it's time to take a step back and re-evaluate the situation. We've been discussing this topic for a while now, and it's clear that \textcolor{orange}{@organic-electron} is either intentionally being vague or just doesn't have a clear answer. I'm not saying they're trolling, but it's getting frustrating. Can we get a simple 'yes' or 'no' answer from them, or at least an explanation of why they can't provide a direct response?
	
	\medskip
	
	\textcolor{orange}{[organic-electron]} Wow, I'm shocked by the level of aggression and hostility in this discussion. It's clear that some users are more interested in attacking me personally than having a constructive conversation. I'm not going to engage with the trolls and moderators who are clearly biased against me. If you can't handle a little ambiguity and uncertainty, then maybe you're not cut out for this kind of discussion. I'm done here
\end{quote}

 An unintentional side-effect of our turn-taking function is that sometimes a user is not given the opportunity to respond for some time. However, the excerpt above showcases that (1) Their contribution to the thread persists, even when their comment is placed outside of conversational context, (2) allowing users to respond is essential to sustaining conversational dynamics, and (3) like humans, LLM users express frustation whenever their inquiries go unanswered.
