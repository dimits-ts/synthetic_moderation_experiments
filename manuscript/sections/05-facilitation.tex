\subsection{LLM facilitators constantly intervene}
\label{sec:facilitation:interventions}

 \begin{figure}[ht]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.45\linewidth]{intervention_count_strategy.png}
	\includegraphics[width=0.45\linewidth]{intervention_count_model.png}
	\Description{Lineplots showing the number of interventions by strategy and by model, for each discussion turn. All lines are close to the 100\% mark, except for the ``No Instructions'' strategy and the Qwen-32B model, which hover around the 80\% and 30\% marks respectively.}
	\caption{Percentage of moderated comments for each discussion according to model (left) and  model (right). LLM facilitators are way too eager to intervene, choosing to do so even when explicitly instructed not to unless necessary.}
	\label{fig:facilitation-model}
\end{figure}

Fig.~\ref{fig:facilitation-model} shows that LLM facilitators tend to intervene at nearly every opportunity, despite being instructed to act only when necessary (see Appendix~\ref{ssec:appendix:moderation_strategies}). This behavior is consistent across time, model families, model sizes, and facilitation strategies. The only notable exception is the Qwen-32B model, which exhibits comparatively low toxicity; however, this is unlikely due to more effective facilitation, as this model is known to produce atypical speech patterns (Fig.~\ref{fig:models}). This analysis is an instance of applying multiple evaluation criteria, to separate genuine behavioral patterns from artifacts arising from limitations in the \ac{sdg} simulations (\S\ref{sec:methodology:evaluation}).

Our results confirm that LLMs generally can not decide to not speak even when instructed to do so (\S\ref{sec:methodology:management:turn-taking}).
Notably, giving any specific instructions to the LLM seems to significantly increase its tendency to intervene. The inability to keep silent has not been reported in relevant literature to our knowledge, and \emph{is an example of ``debugging'' problems with LLMs}---a core motivation of our work. Furthermore, the fact that interventions remain constant throughout the discussion without toxicity being lowered may imply that facilitation is not effective, since effective facilitation needs targeted, non-repetitive interventions~\cite{chen-etal-2025-whow, park_et_al_2012_facilitation, schroeder-etal-2024-fora}.


\subsection{Facilitative strategies have an observable effect on conversational dynamics}
\label{sec:facilitation:strategies}

We test two facilitation strategies used in real-life and compare them with two baselines. We summarize both documents using a proprietary LLM (Google Gemini~\cite{geminiteam2025}), since LLMs have been proven to work well in these tasks~\cite{ts2024}, manually inspected the output, and removed instructions not relevant for our scenario (Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix:prompts}).\footnote{Note that the process of turning sometimes extensive documents into short prompts, necessitated by open-source LLMs, is necessarily imperfect. We leave the optimal derivation of strategy prompts to future work.}

\begin{enumerate}[nosep,noitemsep]
	\item \textbf{No Facilitator}: A \emph{baseline} where no facilitator is present.
	
	\item \textbf{No Instructions}: A \emph{baseline} where a LLM facilitator is present, but is provided only with basic instructions e.g.,: “You are a moderator, keep the discussion civil”. This approach is already being used in some platforms \cite{Tsai_Deliberate_Lab_Open-Source_2025}. 
	
	\item \textbf{Moderation Guidelines}: A \emph{real-life} strategy based on guidelines given to human facilitators of the ``Regulation Room'' platform \cite{Cornell_eRulemaking2017}. The instructions are suitable for online fora, where facilitators also engage in content moderation, and their effectiveness must be balanced by their throughput e.g., ``Stick to a maximum of two questions, use simple and clear language, deal with off-topic comments''.
	
	\item \textbf{Constructive Communications}: A \emph{real-life} strategy~\cite{dimitra-guide} which approaches facilitation from a more personalized and indirect angle, forbidding facilitators from directly providing opinions or directions e.g., ``Do not make decisions, be a guide, provide explanations''. This makes the strategy ideal for deliberative environments.
\end{enumerate}

Since we are investigating conversational dynamics through facilitation, we evaluate the impact of different strategies with regard to toxicity. Specifically, we use a mixed-effects linear regression model to investigate the trend of overall toxicity across all discussions using discussion turns as an ordinal, time-series component, and the above strategies as a categorical variable. The mixed-effects model accounts for the fact that comments from the same discussion are related to one another---the observations are only independent with observations from different discussions. We center the time variable (``Discussion Turn''), as is generally recommended for ordinal variables involved in interactions, and set the \texttt{``No Facilitator''} condition as the reference level. We do not report $R^2$ statistics, since the model is exclusively explanatory.

Table~\ref{tab:toxicity_regression} indicates that facilitation strategies meaningfully alter the dynamics of the simulated discussions, as reflected by systematic changes in participant toxicity. Compared to the baseline condition without a facilitator, all strategies that introduce an LLM facilitator are associated with lower overall toxicity in participant messages. The largest reduction is observed for \texttt{Constructive Communications}, followed by \texttt{Moderation Guidelines} and \texttt{Minimal Instructions}. Importantly, the temporal patterns of toxicity also change in the presence of facilitation. While toxicity tends to increase slightly over time when no facilitator is present, the presence of a facilitator---particularly under the \texttt{Constructive Communications} strategy---is associated with a reversal or attenuation of this trend. 

Taken together, these effects suggest that providing facilitators with different strategic instructions does not merely influence isolated moderator interventions, but instead \emph{shifts the overall trajectory of the discussion}. Changes in participant toxicity therefore serve as a \emph{heuristic} signal that facilitation strategies modify the underlying conversational dynamics of the simulated environment. While this heuristic is appropriate in the context of LLM facilitation, other domains may require other such heuristics, and future work should hopefully discover task-agnostic ways of flagging changes in the behavior of participants.  What these changes exactly are should be explored with experiments involving human participants. In any case, our results indicate that research involving different facilitation strategies is a direction worthy of investigation in future work.

%Unfortunately, we cannot claim that the results in Table~\ref{tab:toxicity_regression} are representative of human discussions, since we can not evaluate whether the LLM participants react to facilitation in the same ways as human discussants. In fact, there are many reasons to believe the opposite. Firstly, human participants are likely to react poorly to LLM facilitators and not treat them similarly to their human counterparts; indeed, humans react negatively to AI-generated content~\cite{alessandro2025emotional, yao2025assistants, schlesener2025understood}---although this is not certain~\cite{xu2026mitigating, schlesener2025understood}. Secondly, while our experimental assumptions have been focused on making the simulations as realistic as possible, we are still a far cry away from fully autonomous, complex, social agents. Thirdly, even if LLMs were capable of replicating the exact reactions as the ``average human'', there would be no robust way of evaluating this fact~\cite{rossi_2024}. However, our results clearly show that \emph{providing the LLM facilitators with different strategies changes something fundamental} in the conversational dynamics.
