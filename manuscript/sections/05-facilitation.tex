\subsection{Synthetic Discussion Generation}
\label{ssec:appendix:discussion}

We use a set of starting comments taken from random points in escalated discussions featured in the CMV-Awry dataset \cite{chang-danescu-niculescu-mizil-2019-trouble}. We then run $N_d=20$ discussions for each possible configuration of LLMs, turn-taking function, and facilitation strategy. Each discussion is composed of up to $30$ comments, including facilitator interventions.

We use different LLMs for participants (user-LLMs) and facilitators (mod-LLMs). While experimenting with different-sized LLMs we noticed that smaller LLMs (3-4B parameters) actually generated more varied, animated and direct speech, while larger models (8B-70B) respond in a more formulaic manner. Since the task of simulating a discussion is much easier compared to facilitating one, we decided to follow the paradigm proposed by~\cite{ulmer2024}, where a large model manages a scenario with specific goals and guidelines, while smaller models are used exclusively to interact with the former.
An overview of how the experiments are generated can be found in Alg.~\ref{alg:exp_generation}.

\begin{algorithm}[ht]
	\caption{Experimental procedure for the facilitation experiments using our \ac{sdg} framework.}
	\label{alg:exp_generation}
	\begin{flushleft}
		\textbf{Input:}
		\begin{itemize}
			\item \textit{User modeling:} user SDBs $\Theta$, number of participants $N_{users}$
			\item \textit{Moderation:} strategies $S$
			\item \textit{Seeding:} seed discussions $D_{\text{seed}}$, number of seed comments $N_{\text{seed}}$
			\item \textit{Models:} LLMs $llms$
			\item \textit{Experiment control:} number of experiments $N_e$, discussion length $N_d$
		\end{itemize}
		\textbf{Output:} set of generated discussions $D$
	\end{flushleft}
	
	\begin{algorithmic}[1]
		\State $D \gets \emptyset$
		
		\ForAll{$llm \in llms$'}
		\ForAll{$strat \in S$}
		\For{$1,\ldots,N_e$}
		
		\State $users \gets$ \Call{InitializeParticipants}{$llm, \Theta, N_{users}$}
		\State $mod \gets$ \Call{InitializeMod}{$llm, strat$}
		\State $d \gets$ \Call{InitializeDiscussion}{$D_{\text{seed}}, N_{\text{seed}}$}
		
		\For{$1,\ldots,N_d$}
		\State $last\_user \gets$ \Call{LastUser}{$d$}
		\State $user \gets$ \Call{NextSpeaker}{$users, last\_user$}
		\State $d \gets d \cup$ \Call{Post}{$user, d$}
		\State $d \gets d \cup$ \Call{Post}{$mod, d, strat$}
		\EndFor
		
		\State $D \gets D \cup \{d\}$
		\EndFor
		\EndFor
		\EndFor
		
		\State \Return $D$
	\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}



\subsection{Facilitation Strategies}
\label{ssec:experimental:strategies}

We test two facilitation strategies used in real-life and compare them with two baselines. We summarize both documents using a proprietary LLM (Google Gemini), since LLMs have been proven to work well in these tasks~\cite{ts2024}, manually inspected the output, and removed instructions not relevant for our scenario.\footnote{Note that the process of turning sometimes extensive documents into short prompts, necessitated by open-source LLMs, is necessarily imperfect. We leave the optimal derivation of strategy prompts to future work.}

\begin{enumerate}[nosep,noitemsep]
	\item \textbf{\strategynomod}: A \emph{baseline} where no facilitator is present.
	
	\item \textbf{\strategynoinstr}: A \emph{baseline} where a LLM facilitator is present, but is provided only with basic instructions e.g.,: “You are a moderator, keep the discussion civil”. This approach is already being used in some platforms \cite{Tsai_Deliberate_Lab_Open-Source_2025}. 
	
	\item \textbf{\strategyregroom}: A \emph{real-life} strategy based on guidelines given to human facilitators of the ``Regulation Room'' platform \cite{Cornell_eRulemaking2017}. The instructions are suitable for online fora, where facilitators also engage in content moderation, and their effectiveness must be balanced by their throughput e.g., ``Stick to a maximum of two questions, use simple and clear language, deal with off-topic comments''.
	
	\item \textbf{\strategyconstrcomm}: A \emph{real-life} strategy~\cite{dimitra-guide} which approaches facilitation from a more personalized and indirect angle, forbidding facilitators from directly providing opinions or directions e.g., ``Do not make decisions, be a guide, provide explanations''. This makes the strategy ideal for deliberative environments.
\end{enumerate}



\subsection{Technical Details}
\label{ssec:experimental:setup}

All experiments were collectively completed within five days of computational time, using two Quadro RTX 6000 GPUs. Toxicity evaluation was conducted using the Perspective API service. The execution script is available in the project's repository. We provide the $h=3$ last comments for context.

We examined five models for facilitation; two large (OLMo-32B, Qwen2.5-32B) and three smaller (LlaMa-8B, OLMo-7B and Qwen2.5-7B). While we experimented with smaller models, they could not handle the size of the input (context + instructions). For the participant agents, we used three smaller models (Gemma-4B and LlaMa-3B, Qwen2.5-4B), but dropped the latter due to degenerate output. All models are quantized to 4bits (\texttt{unsloth, bnb} variants).


\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{LLM facilitators constantly intervene}

 \begin{figure}[ht]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.45\linewidth]{intervention_count_model.png}
	\includegraphics[width=0.45\linewidth]{intervention_count_strategy.png}
	\caption{Percentage of unmoderated comments for each discussion according to model (left) and facilitation strategy (right). LLM facilitators are way too eager to intervene, choosing to do so even when explicitly instructed not to unless necessary.}
	\label{fig:interventions}
\end{figure}

Fig.~\ref{fig:interventions} demonstrates that LLM facilitators intervene at almost any opportunity,  even though they are instructed to only do so when necessary (see \ref{ssec:appendix:moderation_strategies}). This confirms that LLMs generally can not decide not to speak even when instructed to do so (\S\ref{ssec:methodology:us}). This behavior is consistent across model families, model sizes and facilitative instructions. This has not been reported in relevant literature to our knowledge, and \emph{is an example of ``debugging'' problems with LLMs}---a core motivation of our work.


\subsection{LLM facilitators cannot effectively utilize complex instructions}

\input{../graphs/toxicity_regression.tex}

We used a mixed-effects linear regression model, which unlike regular linear regression, accounts for the fact that comments from the same discussion tend to resemble one another.  Table~\ref{tab:regression} shows that, while complex instructions lead to statistically significant reductions in message toxicity, the effects are quantitatively minimal. This can be seen both in aggregate (with toxicity dropping only by $0.018-0.0019$ points when compared to no instructions), and in continuity (no effect through time).


\subsection{Troll users enhance conversational dynamics}



Fig.~\ref{fig:trolls_toxicity} shows the difference in toxicity exhibited by normal users (excluding trolls and facilitators) when increasing the number of troll users. In general, the more trolls are in a discussion, the more normal users get goaded into adopting toxic posturing. There is a notable exception to this pattern; when a single troll is present, toxicity does not increase in a statistically significant way. We have not found any fault in our experimental procedure that explains this; rather we posit that it reflects a new conversational dynamic also encountered in human discussions.

When a single troll is present, we may observe collective action from the rest of the participants, who support the facilitator while he intervenes against the troll. In human discussions, this would be observed as collective group action against a perceived threat; in online communities, against trolls~\cite{sun2021would,jakubowicz2017cyber,coles2016trolling}. While discussions with no trolls may devolve into bickering, the collective focus of the participants to shut down the troll tends to unify them, reducing overall toxicity between them (see Table~\ref{tab:flag} for an example of such a discussion). This pattern has been shown to increase community cohesion in human discussions~\cite{hopkinson2013trolling}. When more than one trolls exist in a discussion, the trolls can not be contained effectively, escalating overall toxicity. While we cannot qualitatively prove this for the entire dataset, since it is composed of many hundreds of discussions and observing behavioral patterns is inherently subjective, we confirm the presence of similar discussions to Table~\ref{tab:flag} across multiple discussions.

While can not make the claim that LLMs follow these human behaviors(\S\ref{ssec:related:human-llm}), this is an example of basic conversational dynamics that we want to capture, and are only possible by introducing new components to our \ac{sdg} methodology (\S\ref{ssec:methodology:design}).  Without the inclusion of trolls, we would likely not have any data on how the LLM facilitator would react to the presence of collective action supporting them.
