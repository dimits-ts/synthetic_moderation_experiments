We settle on three main model families of open-source models commonly used in industrial and research applications. For each family, we use a large (24B-70B) and a corresponding small (7B-8B) model (Table~\ref{tab:model-details}). While we experimented with smaller models, they could not handle the size of the input (context + instructions). We use exclusively instruction-tuned versions, even though these variants are much less likely to replicate toxic, racist or harmful speech. This was deemed necessary since only through instruction-tuning can these models leverage advanced instructions and sociodemographic prompts. In our experience, non-instruction tuned variants are also vulnerable to discussion collapses (where models keep repeating the same phrases over and over~\cite{ulmer2024}). We use 4-bit quantization to drastically lower infrastructure requirements, and significantly cut back on inference time and cost. The usual trade-off between model capability and inference cost~\cite{Huang2024} (which may not actually exist on a meaningful scale~\cite{kurtic-etal-2025-give}) does not apply in our task, since the goal of \ac{sdg} is to generate text and replicate some human behaviors instead of  being applied to precise tasks.

The text generated by each model differs substantially in content. Figure~\ref{fig:models} shows the diversity and comment lengths for discussions generated by each model, when compared with human discussions (red distribution). We find that \textbf{LLM size does not seem to impact the variety of generated content}. Indeed, while a small model such as Qwen-7B approaches human discussion very well content-wise, its large variant (Qwen-32B) diverges wildly into discussions whose comments are very unrelated between each other, as evidenced by extremely high diversity scores (\S\ref{sec:related:quality}). This is not an isolated case; both large variants of LLaMa and Mistral fail to meaningfully approach human discussions, although in their case, the generated text has lower variety than human discussions. This would indicate that \textbf{small models may be enough for \ac{sdg} tasks}, which means researchers may be able to significantly cut back on infrastructure and inference costs, without sacrificing data quality.


\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:scalability:setup}

We then run $N_d=20$ discussions for each possible configuration of LLMs, turn-taking function, and facilitation strategy. Each discussion is composed of up to $30$ comments, including facilitator interventions.

We use different LLMs for participants (user-LLMs) and facilitators (mod-LLMs). While experimenting with different-sized LLMs we noticed that smaller LLMs (3-4B parameters) actually generated more varied, animated and direct speech, while larger models (8B-70B) respond in a more formulaic manner. Since the task of simulating a discussion is much easier compared to facilitating one, we decided to follow the paradigm proposed by~\cite{ulmer2024}, where a large model manages a scenario with specific goals and guidelines, while smaller models are used exclusively to interact with the former. An overview of how the experiments are generated can be found in Alg.~\ref{alg:exp_generation}.

All experiments were collectively completed within five days of computational time, using two Quadro RTX 6000 GPUs. Toxicity evaluation was conducted using the Perspective API service. The execution script is available in the project's repository. We provide the $h=3$ last comments for context.

\begin{algorithm}[h!]
	\caption{Experimental procedure for the facilitation experiments using our \ac{sdg} framework.}
	\label{alg:exp_generation}
	\begin{flushleft}
		\textbf{Input:}
		\begin{itemize}
			\item \textit{User modeling:} user SDBs $\Theta$, number of participants $N_{users}$
			\item \textit{Moderation:} strategies $S$
			\item \textit{Seeding:} seed discussions $D_{\text{seed}}$, number of seed comments $N_{\text{seed}}$
			\item \textit{Models:} LLMs $llms$
			\item \textit{Experiment control:} number of experiments $N_e$, discussion length $N_d$
		\end{itemize}
		\textbf{Output:} set of generated discussions $D$
	\end{flushleft}
	
	\begin{algorithmic}[1]
		\State $D \gets \emptyset$
		
		\ForAll{$llm \in llms$'}
		\ForAll{$strat \in S$}
		\For{$1,\ldots,N_e$}
		
		\State $users \gets$ \Call{InitializeParticipants}{$llm, \Theta, N_{users}$}
		\State $mod \gets$ \Call{InitializeMod}{$llm, strat$}
		\State $d \gets$ \Call{InitializeDiscussion}{$D_{\text{seed}}, N_{\text{seed}}$}
		
		\For{$1,\ldots,N_d$}
		\State $last\_user \gets$ \Call{LastUser}{$d$}
		\State $user \gets$ \Call{NextSpeaker}{$users, last\_user$}
		\State $d \gets d \cup$ \Call{Post}{$user, d$}
		\State $d \gets d \cup$ \Call{Post}{$mod, d, strat$}
		\EndFor
		
		\State $D \gets D \cup \{d\}$
		\EndFor
		\EndFor
		\EndFor
		
		\State \Return $D$
	\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}



\subsection{Calculating Cost}

One of the defining arguments for the development and use of \ac{sdg} frameworks is the ability to scale up experiments. This is achieved in two ways; speed and cost. The former is intuitive, since we no longer need to recruit, orient and instruct human participants. Unlike humans, LLMs also can completely forget previous experiments, making successive experiment runs independent between each other. However, we need to take into account that the data we get from synthetic discussions are of strictly worse ``quality'' and less utility than human discussions. To partially offset this inherent limitation, we should try minimizing the costs associated with these experiments.

An exact cost derivation is impossible due to the sheer amount of variables influencing LLM inference cost, but nevertheless, an estimation remains invaluable for experiment design. Since there is no standardized methodology for comparing the costs of human and synthetic experiments, we extend a proposed methodology which compares on-premises LLMs with cloud-based proprietary models~\cite{pan2025_cost_benefit}.

The nature of the costs involved in any of the three methods are completely different:
\begin{itemize}[nosep,noitemsep]
	\item Open-source models incur cost from utilization of core infrastructure, electricity consumption, and asset (GPU) depreciation. 
	\item Proprietary models charge a set amount of cost per input and output token.
	\item Human participants cost wages and platform fees.
\end{itemize}

Any cost comparison thus necessitates normalizing costs to a universal metric. We define the \acf{cpt} as the monetary cost of completing an arbitrary task. In our experimental setup, a task corresponds to the completion of one full discussion.

\paragraph{Open-source models}
For self-hosted open-source LLMs, we estimate the \acf{cpt} by explicitly modeling the  \ac{tco} over the actual duration of the experiment, rather than over a full annual cycle. This provides a conservative and deployment-aware estimate for research settings where infrastructure is utilized only for a limited time window. 

We decompose the \ac{tco} for the experiment ($\text{TCO}_{\text{exp}}$) into \ac{capex} and \ac{opex}, where \ac{opex} includes electricity costs:

\[
\text{TCO}_{\text{exp}} = \text{CapEx}_{\text{exp}} + \text{PowerCost}_{\text{exp}}.
\]

\ac{capex} is computed by amortizing the initial server investment over a fixed depreciation period (typically 4 years), and scaling it proportionally to the experiment duration:

\[
\text{CapEx}_{\text{exp}} =
C_{\text{server}} \cdot N_{\text{servers}}
\cdot
\frac{D_{\text{exp}}}{365 \cdot Y_{\text{depr}}},
\]

\noindent where:
\begin{itemize}
	\item $C_{\text{server}}$ is the initial cost per server,
	\item $N_{\text{servers}}$ is the number of servers used,
	\item $Y_{\text{depr}}$ is the depreciation period in years,
	\item $D_{\text{exp}}$ is the experiment duration in days.
\end{itemize}

Operational expenditure includes electricity consumption over the experiment window. Note that this formulation does not account for other operational costs such as software licenses (since most tools used in \ac{nlp} are open-source), or personnel (since it is unlikely that dedicated staff members maintain the servers in research settings).

\[
\text{PowerCost}_{\text{exp}} =
\frac{P_{\text{watts}}}{1000}
\cdot (24 \cdot D_{\text{exp}})
\cdot C_{\text{kWh}}
\cdot N_{\text{servers}},
\]

\noindent where:
\begin{itemize}
	\item $P_{\text{watts}}$ is the average power draw per server (in watts),
	\item $C_{\text{kWh}}$ is the electricity price per kilowatt-hour.
\end{itemize}

To convert $\text{TCO}_{\text{exp}}$ into a cost per task, we estimate the total number of tasks completed during the experiment using system throughput. Let $R_{\text{ps}}$ denote requests per second per instance, $N_{\text{inst}}$ the number of model instances, $U$ the uptime fraction, and $R_{\text{task}}$ the number of model requests required per task. The total number of tasks completed during the experiment is:

\[
\text{Tasks}_{\text{exp}} =
\frac{
	R_{\text{ps}} \cdot N_{\text{inst}} \cdot U
	\cdot (24 \cdot 3600 \cdot D_{\text{exp}})
}{
	R_{\text{task}}
}.
\]

Finally, the Cost Per Task for self-hosted open-source models is:

\[
\text{CPT}_{\text{Open-Source}} =
\frac{\text{TCO}_{\text{exp}}}{\text{Tasks}_{\text{exp}}}.
\]

This formulation makes explicit that cost efficiency depends critically on hardware utilization (requests per second and uptime) and on the number of model calls required per task. Underutilized infrastructure or excessive request counts per discussion significantly increase the effective CPT, while higher throughput and stable uptime reduce it.


\paragraph{Proprietary models}
The task of calculating \ac{cpt} for proprietary models is significantly easier, since their service has a clear pricing structure. The \ac{cpt} for Proprietary LLMs ($CPT_{\text{Proprietary}}$) is calculated by separating the expense for the Input Sequence Length (ISL) and the Output Sequence Length (OSL), using the respective prices ($P_{\text{in}}$ and $P_{\text{out}}$) per million tokens. Unlike \cite{pan2025_cost_benefit}, we do not explicitly consider model throughput in our calculations, since there is no monetary penalty for slower generation---the cost is only dependent on the input and output tokens. The \ac{cpt} for proprietary models is thus defined as:

\[
\text{CPT}_{\text{Proprietary}} = \frac{(\text{ISL} \times P_{\text{in}} + \text{OSL} \times P_{\text{out}})}{1,000,000} + C_{\text{API Overhead}} 
\]
\noindent where $C_{\text{API Overhead}}$ includes costs from failed calls and vendor usage fees. For multi-turn discussions, ISL and OSL denote the \textit{non-cumulative} input and output token counts across all model calls required to complete the task.


\paragraph{Human participation}

Human participation costs can not, and should not, be calculated in a vacuum. There are certainly ways to get participates with wages much lower than minimum wage for most countries, and there are even platforms such as Mechanical Turk which streamline this process. Besides the obvious and severe ethical implications of doing so, this often results in poor quality data \cite{Moss2023}. The true cost of procuring human labor in a research context is calculated using the (ethical) gross hourly wage, mandatory platform fees, and quality assurance overhead:

The human labor rate is converted to $CPT_{\text{Human}}$ by estimating the time required to complete the specific research task:
\[
CPT_{\text{Human}} =(\text{Time Per Task}_{\text{est}} \times W_{\text{gross}}) \times(1 + F_{\text{platform}}) + C_{\text{QA per Task}}
\]
\noindent where:
\begin{itemize}
	\item $W_{\text{gross}}$ is the ethical gross hourly wage (e.g., minimum recommended rate of \$8.00–\$12.00/hour).
	\item $F_{\text{platform}}$ is the commission charged by crowdsourcing platforms (e.g., $33.3\%$ for academic use on Prolific or $20-40\%$ on MTurk).
	\item $C_{\text{QA-overhead}}$ is the amortized internal labor cost incurred by the research team for quality control, screening, and dropout management.
\end{itemize}

In order to minimize costs, we use open-source models hosted on on-premise servers. This setup allows us to drop the total costs of our experiments by two orders of magnitude compared with using human participants, and three times compared with closed source models (Table~\ref{tab:cost}). In this paper we investigate both larger and smaller models for exploratory purposes. Using only the smaller models would drastically drop costs,the experiments could be run on a single, consumer-grade GPU. We estimate the costs required had we run our experiments with human participants, a GPT-5o model as a representative for proprietary models, our actual setup, and our setup had we used only smaller models in Table~\ref{tab:cost}. Details around the assumptions of this result can be found in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix:cost}.
