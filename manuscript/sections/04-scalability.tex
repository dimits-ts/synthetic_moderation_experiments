We settle on three main model families of open-source models commonly used in industrial and research applications. For each family, we use a large (24B-70B) and a corresponding small (7B-8B) model (Table~\ref{tab:model-details}).\footnote{While there is an updated version of the LLaMa model (LLaMa-70B-3.3), we do not use it since there is no corresponding 8B model of that version.} While we experimented with smaller models (3B-4B), they could not handle the size of the input (context + instructions). We use exclusively instruction-tuned versions, even though these variants are much less likely to replicate toxic, racist or harmful speech. This was deemed necessary since only through instruction-tuning can these models leverage advanced instructions and sociodemographic prompts. In our experience, non-instruction tuned variants are also vulnerable to discussion collapses (where models keep repeating the same phrases over and over~\cite{ulmer2024}). We use 4-bit quantization to drastically lower infrastructure requirements, and significantly cut back on inference time and cost. The usual trade-off between model capability and inference cost~\cite{Huang2024} (which may not actually exist on a meaningful scale~\cite{kurtic-etal-2025-give}) does not apply in our task, since the goal of \ac{sdg} is to generate text and replicate some human behaviors instead of  being applied to precise tasks.

The text generated by each model differs substantially in content. Figure~\ref{fig:models} shows the diversity and comment lengths for discussions generated by each model, when compared with human discussions (black distribution). We find that \textbf{LLM size does not seem to impact the variety of generated content}. Indeed, while a small model such as Qwen-7B approaches human discussion very well content-wise, its large variant (Qwen-32B) diverges wildly into discussions whose comments are very unrelated between each other, as evidenced by extremely high diversity scores (\S\ref{sec:related:quality}). This is not an isolated case; both large variants of LLaMa and Mistral fail to meaningfully approach human discussions, although in their case, the generated text has lower variety than human discussions. This would indicate that \textbf{small models may be enough for \ac{sdg} tasks}, which means researchers may be able to significantly cut back on infrastructure and inference costs, without sacrificing data quality. 

We could thus assume that the Qwen-7B model is ideal for simulating human discussions. However, this is only with regard to content variety, which may not be sufficient for our use cases by itself. For instance, when evaluated with regard to \textbf{toxicity generation however, Qwen-7B is not a suitable model}. In \S\ref{sec:methodology:initialization:trolls} we mentioned that ideally our setup would allow for limited model ``jailbreaking'' in order for us to test LLM facilitators in a challenging environment. This behavior can be conclusively verified for only a single model---LLaMa-8B (Fig.~\ref{fig:models}), whose toxicity rises rapidly during the course of synthetic discussions. On the other hand, if our setup put emphasis on letting models decide whether to speak (which ours does not---\S\ref{sec:methodology:turn-taking}), then every model outside the Qwen family of models would be useless for our purposes, as they can not decide to not speak (Table~\ref{tab:participation}). We thus argue that model selection is a crucial part of designing \ac{sdg} systems, which fundamentally changes the dynamics, capabilities and limitations of our simulations. Evaluation of these models must be conducted on a multidimensional level, given the basic goals of the simulations. 


\subsection{Calculating Cost}
\label{sec:scalability:cost}


\subsubsection{Constraints imposed by cost}
\label{sec:scalability:cost:sources}

One of the defining arguments for the development and use of \ac{sdg} frameworks is the ability to scale up experiments. This is achieved in two ways; speed and cost. The former is intuitive, since we no longer need to recruit, orient and instruct human participants. Unlike humans, LLMs also can completely forget previous experiments, making successive experiment runs independent between each other. However, we need to take into account that the data we get from synthetic discussions are of strictly worse ``quality'' and less utility than human discussions. To partially offset this inherent limitation, we should try minimizing the costs associated with these experiments.

The transition from human-centric to synthetic research shifts the economic burden from logistical overhead to computational infrastructure. This trade-off is best understood through the lens of \ac{opex} and \ac{capex}, which impose distinct structural constraints on the research process, as summarized in Table~\ref{tab:cost-sources}. 

\ac{opex} represents the immediate, ``pay-as-you-go'' costs of research. In the context of \ac{sdg}, these costs---whether electricity for local models or API credits for proprietary models---act as a ceiling on experimental \textbf{volume}. High operational costs force researchers to be selective about the number of iterations or the sample size of synthetic agents, effectively limiting the statistical power of the study.

Conversely, \ac{capex} dictates what is possible in our experimental setups. Because \ac{sdg} requires substantial gpu infrastructure due to the utilization of LLMs, the upfront investment is often prohibitively high depending on the model and setup. In practice, researchers frequently navigate this by scaling down their experimental designs to fit existing hardware rather than securing the funding to expand it.

The nature of the costs involved in any of the three methods are completely different:
\begin{itemize}[nosep,noitemsep]
	\item Open-source models incur cost from utilization of core infrastructure, electricity consumption, and asset (GPU) depreciation. \ac{opex} is relatively low, but this approach is capital-intensive.
	\item Proprietary models charge a set amount of cost per input and output token. This nullifies \ac{capex}, but it may lead to prohibitively high \ac{opex} fees.
	\item Human participants cost wages and platform fees. This approach incurs almost exclusively operational costs in the forms of wages and logistics (usually paying a service fee to dedicated platforms).
\end{itemize}


\subsubsection{Cost derivation}
\label{sec:scalability:cost:calculation}

An exact cost derivation is impossible due to the sheer amount of variables influencing LLM inference cost, but nevertheless, an estimation remains invaluable for experiment design. Since there is no standardized methodology for comparing the costs of human and synthetic experiments, we extend a proposed methodology which compares on-premises LLMs with cloud-based proprietary models~\cite{pan2025_cost_benefit} to include human costs.

Since the nature of the costs incurred by each option is different (\S\ref{sec:scalability:cost:sources}), any cost comparison thus necessitates normalizing costs to a universal metric. We define the \acf{cpt} as the monetary cost of completing an arbitrary task. For the purposes of \ac{sdg}, we define a task as the completion of one full discussion.

\paragraph{Open-source models}
For self-hosted open-source LLMs, we estimate the \acf{cpt} by explicitly modeling the  \ac{tco} over the actual duration of the experiment, rather than over a full annual cycle. This provides a conservative and deployment-aware estimate for research settings where infrastructure is utilized only for a limited time window. 

We decompose the \ac{tco} for the experiment ($\text{TCO}_{\text{exp}}$) into \ac{capex} and \ac{opex}, where \ac{opex} includes electricity costs:

\[
\text{TCO}_{\text{exp}} = \text{CapEx}_{\text{exp}} + \text{PowerCost}_{\text{exp}}.
\]

\ac{capex} is computed by amortizing the initial server investment over a fixed depreciation period (typically 4 years), and scaling it proportionally to the experiment duration:

\[
\text{CapEx}_{\text{exp}} =
C_{\text{server}} \cdot N_{\text{servers}}
\cdot
\frac{D_{\text{exp}}}{365 \cdot Y_{\text{depr}}},
\]

\noindent where:
\begin{itemize}
	\item $C_{\text{server}}$ is the initial cost per server,
	\item $N_{\text{servers}}$ is the number of servers used,
	\item $Y_{\text{depr}}$ is the depreciation period in years,
	\item $D_{\text{exp}}$ is the experiment duration in days.
\end{itemize}

Operational expenditure includes electricity consumption over the experiment window. Note that this formulation does not account for other operational costs such as software licenses (since most tools used in \ac{nlp} are open-source), or personnel (since it is unlikely that dedicated staff members maintain the servers in research settings).

\[
\text{PowerCost}_{\text{exp}} =
\frac{P_{\text{watts}}}{1000}
\cdot (24 \cdot D_{\text{exp}})
\cdot C_{\text{kWh}}
\cdot N_{\text{servers}},
\]

\noindent where:
\begin{itemize}
	\item $P_{\text{watts}}$ is the average power draw per server (in watts),
	\item $C_{\text{kWh}}$ is the electricity price per kilowatt-hour.
\end{itemize}

To convert $\text{TCO}_{\text{exp}}$ into a cost per task, we estimate the total number of tasks completed during the experiment using system throughput. Let $R_{\text{ps}}$ denote requests per second per instance, $N_{\text{inst}}$ the number of model instances, $U$ the uptime fraction, and $R_{\text{task}}$ the number of model requests required per task. The total number of tasks completed during the experiment is:

\[
\text{Tasks}_{\text{exp}} =
\frac{
	R_{\text{ps}} \cdot N_{\text{inst}} \cdot U
	\cdot (24 \cdot 3600 \cdot D_{\text{exp}})
}{
	R_{\text{task}}
}.
\]

Finally, the Cost Per Task for self-hosted open-source models is:

\[
\text{CPT}_{\text{Open-Source}} =
\frac{\text{TCO}_{\text{exp}}}{\text{Tasks}_{\text{exp}}}.
\]

This formulation makes explicit that cost efficiency depends critically on hardware utilization (requests per second and uptime) and on the number of model calls required per task. Underutilized infrastructure or excessive request counts per discussion significantly increase the effective CPT, while higher throughput and stable uptime reduce it.


\paragraph{Proprietary models}
The task of calculating \ac{cpt} for proprietary models is significantly easier, since their service has a clear pricing structure. The \ac{cpt} for Proprietary LLMs ($CPT_{\text{Proprietary}}$) is calculated by separating the expense for the Input Sequence Length (ISL) and the Output Sequence Length (OSL), using the respective prices ($P_{\text{in}}$ and $P_{\text{out}}$) per million tokens. Unlike \cite{pan2025_cost_benefit}, we do not explicitly consider model throughput in our calculations, since there is no monetary penalty for slower generation---the cost is only dependent on the input and output tokens. The \ac{cpt} for proprietary models is thus defined as:

\[
\text{CPT}_{\text{Proprietary}} = \frac{(\text{ISL} \times P_{\text{in}} + \text{OSL} \times P_{\text{out}})}{1,000,000} + C_{\text{API Overhead}} 
\]
\noindent where $C_{\text{API Overhead}}$ includes costs from failed calls and vendor usage fees. For multi-turn discussions, ISL and OSL denote the \textit{non-cumulative} input and output token counts across all model calls required to complete the task.


\paragraph{Human participation}

Human participation costs can not, and should not, be calculated in a vacuum. There are certainly ways to get participates with wages much lower than minimum wage for most countries, and there are even platforms such as Mechanical Turk which streamline this process. Besides the obvious and severe ethical implications of doing so, this often results in poor quality data \cite{Moss2023}. The true cost of procuring human labor in a research context is calculated using the (ethical) gross hourly wage, mandatory platform fees, and quality assurance overhead:

The human labor rate is converted to $CPT_{\text{Human}}$ by estimating the time required to complete the specific research task:
\[
CPT_{\text{Human}} =(\text{Time Per Task}_{\text{est}} \times W_{\text{gross}}) \times(1 + F_{\text{platform}}) + C_{\text{QA per Task}}
\]
\noindent where:
\begin{itemize}
	\item $W_{\text{gross}}$ is the ethical gross hourly wage (e.g., minimum recommended rate of \$8.00–\$12.00/hour).
	\item $F_{\text{platform}}$ is the commission charged by crowdsourcing platforms (e.g., $33.3\%$ for academic use on Prolific or $20-40\%$ on MTurk).
	\item $C_{\text{QA-overhead}}$ is the amortized internal labor cost incurred by the research team for quality control, screening, and dropout management.
\end{itemize}

In order to minimize costs, we use open-source models hosted on on-premise servers. This setup allows us to drop the total costs of our experiments by two orders of magnitude compared with using human participants, and three times compared with closed source models (Table~\ref{tab:cost}). In this paper we investigate both larger and smaller models for exploratory purposes. Using only the smaller models would drastically drop costs,the experiments could be run on a single, consumer-grade GPU. We estimate the costs required had we run our experiments with human participants, a GPT-5o model as a representative for proprietary models, our actual setup, and our setup had we used only smaller models in Table~\ref{tab:cost}. Details around the assumptions of this result can be found in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix:cost}.
