\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{LLM facilitators constantly intervene}

 \begin{figure}[ht]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.45\linewidth]{intervention_count_model.png}
	\includegraphics[width=0.45\linewidth]{intervention_count_strategy.png}
	\caption{Percentage of unmoderated comments for each discussion according to model (left) and facilitation strategy (right). LLM facilitators are way too eager to intervene, choosing to do so even when explicitly instructed not to unless necessary.}
	\label{fig:interventions}
\end{figure}

Fig.~\ref{fig:interventions} demonstrates that LLM facilitators intervene at almost any opportunity,  even though they are instructed to only do so when necessary (see \ref{ssec:appendix:moderation_strategies}). This confirms that LLMs generally can not decide not to speak even when instructed to do so (\S\ref{ssec:methodology:us}). This behavior is consistent across model families, model sizes and facilitative instructions. This has not been reported in relevant literature to our knowledge, and \emph{is an example of ``debugging'' problems with LLMs}---a core motivation of our work.


\subsection{LLM facilitators cannot effectively utilize complex instructions}

\input{../graphs/toxicity_regression.tex}

We investigate the effect of facilitation instructions through a mixed linear model regression analysis. Table~\ref{tab:regression} shows that, while complex instructions lead to statistically significant reductions in message toxicity, the effects are quantitatively minimal. This can be seen both in aggregate (with toxicity dropping only by $0.018-0.0019$ points when compared to no instructions), and in continuity (no effect through time).


\subsection{Troll users enhance conversational dynamics}

 \begin{figure}[ht]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{toxicity_vs_troll_count.png}

	\caption{Average toxicity of non-troll, non-facilitator users across discussions with X numbers of active trolls. Since the chance of a user being assigned the role of troll is fixed, there are few discussions where trolls dominate the user space, leading to large error bars.}
	\label{fig:trolls_toxicity}
\end{figure}

Fig.~\ref{fig:trolls_toxicity} shows the difference in toxicity exhibited by normal users (excluding trolls and facilitators) when increasing the number of troll users. In general, the more trolls are in a discussion, the more normal users get goaded into adopting toxic posturing. There is a notable exception to this pattern; when a single troll is present, toxicity actually drops. We have not found any fault in our experimental procedure that explains it; rather we posit that it reflects a new conversational dynamic.

When a single troll is present, we observe a ``rally-around-the-flag'' effect, where the users  support the facilitator while he intervenes against the troll. While discussions with no trolls may devolve into bickering, the collective focus of the participants to shut down the troll tends to unify them, reducing overall toxicity between them (see Table~\ref{tab:flag} for an example). When more than one trolls exist in a discussion, the trolls can not be contained effectively, escalating overall toxicity. This is an example of basic conversational dynamics that we want to capture, and are only possible by introducing new components to our \ac{sdg} methodology (\S\ref{ssec:methodology:design}).
