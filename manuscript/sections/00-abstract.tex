\begin{abstract}
	Human facilitation of online discussions is insufficiently scalable for modern digital spaces. Large Language Models (LLMs) offer a potential solution by automating facilitation, but large-scale evaluation has been limited due to the high cost of human involvement. To address this, we introduce Synthetic Discussion Generation (SDG), a novel NLP task for creating simulated discussions that enable cost-effective pilot experiments. Drawing on prior SDG systems and interdisciplinary literature, we define design, documentation, implementation, and evaluation guidelines for such simulations. Using these principles, we systematically evaluate three open-source LLM families across multiple dimensions, finding that smaller models (7B–8B) perform effectively. Applying a new cost-calculation methodology, we show that SDG experiments with small models incur costs several orders of magnitude lower than comparable human experiments, ad 50\% less than experiments using proprietary-models such as GPT-5.1. We implement our approach in an open-source Python library, and execute thousands of inexpensive SDG experiments which reveal a severe limitations on the capabilities of LLM facilitators; they cannot decide when to intervene in a discussion, leading to derailment patterns similar to those observed in human discussions. We also find that using different facilitation strategies leads to substantial changes in conversational dynamics. Finally, we release a large, publicly available dataset containing LLM-generated discussions spanning multiple models, providing a resource for facilitator fine-tuning and behavioral analysis of out-of-the-box LLMs in synthetic discussion settings.
\end{abstract}
