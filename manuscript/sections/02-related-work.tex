\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Synthetic Discussions}
\label{ssec:related:discussions}
While studies exist for simulating user interactions in social media \cite{park_simulacra,mou_2024,tornberg_2023,y_social},and for using LLM facilitators \cite{kim_et_al_chatbot,cho-etal-2024-language}, none so far have combined the two approaches. One approach~\cite{balog_2024} extracts topics and comments from online human discussions and prompt an LLM to continue them. However, it does not use LLM-based user agents to simulate conversational dynamics, nor does it include facilitator agents. Another approach~\cite{ulmer2024} creates synthetic discussions between two roles: an agent controlling a fictional environment and a client interacting with it. These discussions are filtered and used to finetune the agent LLM for a specific task. Our methodology generalizes this framework: an agent (facilitator) interacts with multiple clients (non-facilitator users). Our work also takes inspiration from synthetic negotiations involving multiple agents with unique agendas and responsibilities~\cite{abdelnabi_negotiations}, as our discussions also feature agents with different motivations (normal users, trolls) and agents with veto power (facilitators).


\paragraph{LLM Facilitation}

Unlike classification models traditionally used in online platforms, LLMs can actively facilitate discussions \cite{korre2025evaluation}. 
%They can warn users for rule violations \cite{Kumar_AbuHashem_Durumeric_2024}, monitor engagement \cite{schroeder-etal-2024-fora}, aggregate diverse opinions \cite{small-polis-llm}, and provide translations and writing tips---which is especially useful for marginalized groups \cite{Tsai2024Generative}. These capabilities suggest that LLMs may be able to assist or even replace human facilitators in many tasks \cite{small-polis-llm,korre2025evaluation}.
Simple rule-based models can enhance discussions~\cite{kim_et_al_chatbot}, although their approach was largely confined to organizing according to a specific social science (not facilitative) strategy, and balancing user activity. LLM facilitators have been used in human discussions~\cite{cho-etal-2024-language} providing high-quality feedback to users, although they struggle to make them more respectful and cooperative. In contrast to both works, ours uses exclusively LLM participants and LLM facilitators, and tests the latter in an explicitly toxic and challenging environment.


\paragraph{Discussion Quality}
\label{ssec:related:quality}

When creating \acp{sdg} we need to evaluate \emph{synthetic data quality}, as discussions without human involvement tend to deteriorate quickly, often becoming repetitive and low-quality \cite{ulmer2024}. Despite this, methods for quantifying the quality of synthetic data remain limited. One approach~\cite{balog_2024} attempts to solve this through metrics that either depend on human datasets with similar discussion topics or lack theoretical grounding, while another~\cite{kim_et_al_chatbot} assess quality through post-discussion surveys and by measuring lexical diversity. \emph{``Diversity''}~\cite{ulmer2024} is a discussion-level metric called which penalizes repeated text sequences between comments using average pairwise ROUGE-L \cite{lin-2004-rouge} scores. While it suffers from the limitations of ROUGE scores (mainly the use of exact-word matching), it is computationally efficient, explainable, and independent of any specific domain and dataset.


\paragraph{LLMs as Human Subjects}
\label{ssec:related:human-llm}

While there is always a desire for synthetic simulation systems to be ``realistic'' w.r.t. human behavior \cite{grossman_2023,tornberg_2023,argyle2023}, this can not be claimed nor reliably measured by using LLM agents in lieu of humans \cite{rossi_2024}.
% It is true that LLMs have demonstrated complex,emergent social behaviors \cite{park2023game,demarzo_2023,leng_2024,abdelnabi_negotiations,abramski_2023,hewitt2024predicting,park2024generativeagentsimulations1000}. However,
Significant limitations of LLMs remain in the context of Social Science experiments such as behavioral hallucinations \cite{rossi_2024}; sociodemographic, statistical and political biases \cite{anthis_2025,hewitt2024predicting,rossi_2024,Taubenfeld2024SystematicBI}; non-deterministic outputs \cite{atil_2025,bisbee_2023}; and excessive agreeableness \cite{Park2023GenerativeAI,anthis_2025,rossi_2024}. Thus, \emph{no study based on LLM agents} can claim that it generates ``realistic'' discussions.
