\subsection{Design}
\label{ssec:methodology:design}

Many \ac{sdg} frameworks have been proposed in literature; both simple \cite{Tsai_Deliberate_Lab_Open-Source_2025,ulmer2024} and complex \cite{balog_2024,abdelnabi_negotiations,park2023game}. What makes these frameworks widely used by other people has not been explored in literature,despite many such implementations.

\subsubsection{Functionality}
We posit that synthetic discussion methodologies need to necessarily implement the following components: \emph{(1) Context management}---since LLMs are stateless, and need to be fed all relevant information as prompts, \emph{(2) Turn-taking}---as LLMs are trained as chatbot assistants and ``taught'' to always respond, \emph{(3) Instructions given to the LLMs}---which include directions on how the agent should act (see section below for how specific these directions should be), as well as information that differentiate multiple users between each other. Without any of these three components, a \ac{sdg} methodology cannot fulfill its primary function.

\subsubsection{Implementation}
In \ac{cs} there is a widely shared notion that simple systems are almost always better at performing their functions (``Keep It Simple Stupid''---KISS) \cite{beck2000extreme,thomas2025simplicity}, which has been validated in real-world applications \cite{banker1989software,ogheneovo2014relationship}. Following this notion,we establish our first design rule: \emph{(1) The framework must be as simple as possible, while achieving its users' goals}. The violation of this rule could explain issues in other academic \ac{sdg} systems~\cite{balog_2024}. It also could explain the under-performance of Concordia~\cite{Vezhnevets2023GenerativeAM}; a generalized, complex framework which has failed to garner widespread adoption despite efforts to promote it, evidenced by most recent publications creating their own \ac{sdg} frameworks. During our experiments, we also encountered a new limitation: \emph{(2) Complexity is directly related to researcher bias}; each new feature necessarily follows our own expectations with how human discussions work. Indeed, recent work on LLM behaviors~\cite{park2023game} managed to derive interesting insights exactly because it did \emph{not} tamper with the way LLM users interacted. Finally, we add the natural limitation that \emph{(3) \ac{sdg} should be cheap}. The primary reason for designing and using \ac{sdg} methodologies is to bypass the cost of human experiments. As the data obtained from these methodologies are inherently less valuable than those gathered through live human interactions, it is necessary to generate large quantities of data without compromising cost efficiency.


\subsection{Our methodology}
\label{ssec:methodology:us}

\paragraph{Context Management} LLM capabilities have incentivized researchers to create elaborate ways of managing context such as dynamic summarization \cite{balog_2024}, LLM self-critique \cite{yu_2024_fincon},or memory modules \cite{Vezhnevets2023GenerativeAM}. These approaches \emph{should not be used} in \acp{sdg}, as they necessarily discard important information such as writing style, exact wording and tone, are computationally expensive and less transparent/explainable. Instead, we assume that the $h$ most recent comments provide sufficient context for the LLM participants; a technique that works well in the context of discussions \cite{pavlopoulos_2020_toxicity}.

\paragraph{Turn Taking} In online fora users often create ``comment chains'' following-up on responses to their previous comments. For each discussion turn we either allow the previous user to respond (with a $40\%$ probability), or select another random participant ($60\%$). This probability was selected experimentally; larger values tend to create ``debate''-style discussions between only two or three participants, while lower values tend to create scenarios with minimal interaction between them.\footnote{It would be interesting to analyze quantitatively how discussions change when modifying this parameter, but this is outside of the scope of our work.} A facilitator can respond after every comment, or stay silent by emitting an empty string.

\paragraph{Instruction Prompting} We use a standard instruction prompt for the participants instructing them to respond to repeatedly toxic comments. This was a necessary measure to bypass the extreme agreeableness of LLMs (as seen in \S\ref{ssec:results:ablation} and in literature---\cite{park2023game,anthis_2025}) and is an example of the trade-off between complexity and research bias v.s. the need to acquire meaningful data. Facilitators are instructed to respond only when necessary. Following the paradigm presented by \cite{abdelnabi_negotiations}, we also assign antagonistic motivations to a subset of participants (trolls), which leads to increased toxicity and variety in discussions \S\ref{ssec:results:ablation}.

\paragraph{LLM Personas} Including \acp{sdb} (e.g., gender, age and education) in prompts has proven promising in the generation of varied content \cite{burton2024large}. We pick personas from the PERSONA dataset \cite{castricato-etal-2025-persona} (see Appendix~\ref{ssec:app:sdbs} for details), and add them to the instruction prompt of each participant. Using these sociodemographic prompts, we observe that LLM users are able to create and share personal narratives and experiences from the provided information (Fig.~\ref{fig::example}). 


\subsection{Scalability}
\label{ssec:methodologies:cost}

One of the defining arguments for the development and use of \ac{sdg} frameworks is the ability to scale up experiments. This is achieved in two ways; speed and cost. The former is intuitive, since we no longer need to recruit, orient and instruct human participants. Unlike humans, LLMs also can completely forget previous experiments, making successive experiment runs independent between each other. The latter is also mostly achieved by removing human participants from the loop. However, we need to take into account that the data we get from synthetic discussions are of strictly worse ``quality'' and less utility than human discussions. To partially offset this inherent limitation, we should try minimizing the costs associated with these experiments.


In order to minimize costs, we use open-source models hosted on on-premise servers. This setup allows us to drop the total costs of our experiments by two orders of magnitude compared with using a cheap human participants, and three times compared with closed source models (Table~\ref{tab:cost}). Additionally, using smaller, quantized LLMs~(\S\ref{ssec:experimental:setup}) allows us to run experiments on a single, consumer-grade GPU, nullifying the usual restriction of self-hosted LLMs.Note that we use more resources for the experiments in this paper, as our approach is partly exploratory, e.g., using different models for facilitators and users. Consequently, the open-source costs in Table~\ref{tab:cost} can be easily halved in practice.

Details around the methodology and assumptions of this result can be found in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix:cost}. An exact cost derivation is impossible due to the sheer amount of variables influencing LLM inference cost, but nevertheless, an estimation remains invaluable for experiment design.
