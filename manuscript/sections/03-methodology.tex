\subsection{Design}
\label{ssec:methodology:design}

Many \ac{sdg} frameworks have been proposed in literature; both simple \cite{Tsai_Deliberate_Lab_Open-Source_2025,ulmer2024} and complex \cite{balog_2024,abdelnabi_negotiations,park2023game}. What makes these frameworks widely used by other people has not been explored in literature,despite many such implementations. In the field of \ac{cs},systems are designed on two related axes: \emph{functional} requirements,which dictate what the system must do,and \emph{non-functional} requirements,which describe how the system must implement its functionality. We will use this analysis to ``diagnose'' previous systems,and better design our own.

\paragraph{Functionality}
We posit that synthetic discussion methodologies need to necessarily implement the following components: \emph{(1) Context management}---since LLMs are stateless,and need to be fed information as prompts,\emph{(2) Turn-taking}---as LLMs are trained as chatbot assistants,and therefore cannot decide to \emph{not} speak,\emph{(3) Instructions given to the LLMs}---which may need to be diversified in multi-participant discussions. These represent the \emph{functional requirements} of our system.

\paragraph{Implementation}

In \ac{cs} there is a widely shared notion that simple systems are almost always better at performing their functions (``Keep It Simple Stupid''---KISS) \cite{beck2000extreme,thomas2025simplicity},which has been validated in real-world applications \cite{banker1989software,ogheneovo2014relationship}. Following this notion,we establish our first design rule: \emph{(1) The framework must be as simple as possible,while achieving its users' goals}. The violation of this rule could explain the frustrations of \cite{balog_2024} and the under-performance of Concordia~\cite{Vezhnevets2023GenerativeAM}; a generalized,complex framework which has failed to garner widespread adoption despite efforts to promote it,evidenced by most recent publications creating their own \ac{sdg} frameworks.  

During our experiments,we also encountered a new limitation: \emph{(2) Complexity is directly related to researcher bias}; each new feature necessarily follows our own expectations with how human discussions work. The work of \cite{park2023game} managed to derive interesting insights,exactly because it did \emph{not} tamper with the way LLM users interacted.

Finally,we posit the natural limitation that \emph{(3) \ac{sdg} should be cheap}. The primary reason for designing and using \ac{sdg} methodologies is to bypass the cost of human experiments. As the data obtained from these methodologies are inherently less valuable than those gathered through live human interactions,it is necessary to generate large quantities of data without compromising cost efficiency.


\subsection{Cost savings}
\label{ssec:methodologies:cost}

One of the defining arguments for the development and use of our framework is cost savings. It is thus natural that we should compare the price of our experiments with alternative options. An exact cost derivation is of course intractable,due to the sheer amount of variables influencing LLM inference cost,but nevertheless,an estimation remains invaluable when deciding which method to use. We assume that there are three main methods of generating such experiments: using proprietary models,open-source LLMs,and human participants.

In order to minimize costs,we use open-source models hosted on-premise servers. This setup allows us to drop the total costs of our experiments by two orders of magnitude compared with using a GPT-5o model,and three orders of magnitude had we used human participants (Table~\ref{tab:cost}). Details around the methodology and assumptions of this result can be found in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix:cost}.

\subsection{Our methodology}
\label{ssec:methodology:us}

\paragraph{Context Management} LLM capabilities have incentivized researchers to create elaborate ways of managing context such as dynamic summarization \cite{balog_2024},LLM self-critique \cite{yu_2024_fincon},or memory modules \cite{Vezhnevets2023GenerativeAM}. These approaches \emph{should not be used} in \acp{sdg},as they necessarily discard important information such as writing style,exact wording and tone,are computationally expensive and less transparent/explainable. Instead,we assume that the $h$ most recent comments provide sufficient context for the LLM participants; a technique that works well in the context of discussions \cite{pavlopoulos_2020_toxicity}.


\paragraph{Turn Taking} In online fora users often create ``comment chains'' following-up on responses to their previous comments. Thus,for each discussion turn we either allow the previous user to respond (with a $40\%$ probability),or select another random participant ($60\%$). This probability was selected experimentally; larger values tend to create ``debate''-style discussions between only two or three participants,while lower values tend to create scenarios with minimal interaction between them.\footnote{It would be interesting to analyze quantitatively how discussions change when modifying this parameter,but this is outside of the scope of our work.} A facilitator can respond after every comment,or stay silent by emitting an empty string.


\paragraph{Instruction Prompting} We use a standard instruction prompt for the participants instructing them to respond to repeatedly toxic comments. This was a necessary measure to bypass the extreme agreeableness of LLMs (as seen in \S\ref{ssec:results:ablation} and in literature---\cite{park2023game,anthis_2025}) and is an example of the trade-off between complexity and research bias v.s. the need to acquire meaningful data. Facilitators are instructed to respond only when necessary. Following the paradigm presented by \cite{abdelnabi_negotiations},we also assign antagonistic motivations to a subset of participants (trolls),which leads to increased toxicity and variety in discussions \S\ref{ssec:results:ablation}.


\paragraph{LLM Personas} Including \acp{sdb} (e.g.,gender,age and education) in prompts has proven promising in the generation of varied content \cite{burton2024large}. We pick personas from the PERSONA dataset \cite{castricato-etal-2025-persona}.\footnote{We use the public version of the dataset,since we were unable to establish contact with the authors.} Using these sociodemographic prompts,we observe that LLM users are able to create and share personal narratives and experiences from the provided information (Fig.~\ref{fig::example}).
