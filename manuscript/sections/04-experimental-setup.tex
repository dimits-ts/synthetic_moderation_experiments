\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:experimental}

\subsection{Synthetic Discussion Generation}
\label{ssec:appendix:discussion}

We use a set of starting comments taken from random points in escalated discussions featured in the CMV-Awry dataset \cite{chang-danescu-niculescu-mizil-2019-trouble}. We then run $N_d=20$ discussions for each possible configuration of LLMs, turn-taking function, and facilitation strategy. Each discussion is composed of up to $30$ comments, including facilitator interventions.

We use different LLMs for participants (user-LLMs) and facilitators (mod-LLMs). While experimenting with different-sized LLMs we noticed that smaller LLMs (3-4B parameters) actually generated more varied, animated and direct speech, while larger models (8B-70B) respond in a more formulaic manner. Since the task of simulating a discussion is much easier compared to facilitating one, we decided to follow the paradigm proposed by~\cite{ulmer2024}, where a large model manages a scenario with specific goals and guidelines, while smaller models are used exclusively to interact with the former.
An overview of how the experiments are generated can be found in Alg.~\ref{alg:exp_generation}.

\begin{algorithm}[ht]
	\caption{Synthetic discussion generation (\ac{sdg}).}
	\label{alg:exp_generation}
	
	\textbf{Input:}
	User SDBs $\Theta$, strategies $S$, seed discussions $D_{seed}$, 
	LLMs $user\_llms$, $mod\_llms$, experiments $N_e$, 
	number of seed comments $N_{seed}$, discussion length $N_d$
	
	\textbf{Output:} Set of discussions $D$
	
	\begin{algorithmic}[1]
		\State $D \gets \emptyset$
		
		\ForAll{$(user\_llm, mod\_llm, strat) \in user\_llms \times mod\_llms \times S$}
			\For{$1,\ldots,N_e$}
			
			\State $(users, mod) \gets$ \Call{AssignParticipants}{$\Theta, user\_llm, mod\_llm, strat$}
			\State $d \gets $ \Call{InitializeDiscussion}{$D_{seed}, N_{seed}$}
			
			\For{$1,\ldots,N_d$}
				\State $last\_user \gets $ \Call{LastUser}{$d$}
				\State $user \gets$ \Call{Next-Speaker}{users, last\_user}
				\State $d \gets d \cup$ \Call{Post}{user, d}
				\State $d \gets d \cup$ \Call{Post}{mod, d, s} \Comment{Facilitator decides whether to intervene}
			\EndFor
			
			\State $D \gets D \cup \{d\}$
			\EndFor
		\EndFor
		
		\State \Return $D$
	\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}



\subsection{Facilitation Strategies}
\label{ssec:experimental:strategies}

We test two facilitation strategies used in real-life and compare them with two baselines. We summarize both documents using a proprietary LLM (Google Gemini), since LLMs have been proven to work well in these tasks~\cite{ts2024}, manually inspected the output, and removed instructions not relevant for our scenario.\footnote{Note that the process of turning sometimes extensive documents into short prompts, necessitated by open-source LLMs, is necessarily imperfect. We leave the optimal derivation of strategy prompts to future work.}

\begin{enumerate}[nosep,noitemsep]
    \item \textbf{\strategynomod}: A \emph{baseline} where no facilitator is present.

    \item \textbf{\strategynoinstr}: A \emph{baseline} where a LLM facilitator is present, but is provided only with basic instructions e.g.,: “You are a moderator, keep the discussion civil”. This approach is already being used in some platforms \cite{Tsai_Deliberate_Lab_Open-Source_2025}. 

    \item \textbf{\strategyregroom}: A \emph{real-life} strategy based on guidelines given to human facilitators of the ``Regulation Room'' platform \cite{Cornell_eRulemaking2017}. The instructions are suitable for online fora, where facilitators also engage in content moderation, and their effectiveness must be balanced by their throughput e.g., ``Stick to a maximum of two questions, use simple and clear language, deal with off-topic comments''.

    \item \textbf{\strategyconstrcomm}: A \emph{real-life} strategy~\cite{dimitra-guide} which approaches facilitation from a more personalized and indirect angle, forbidding facilitators from directly providing opinions or directions e.g., ``Do not make decisions, be a guide, provide explanations''. This makes the strategy ideal for deliberative environments.
\end{enumerate}



\subsection{Evaluation}
\label{ssec:experimental:evaluation}

In order to gauge the quality of our synthetic discussions, since we can not reliably measure ``realism'' (\S\ref{ssec:related:human-llm}), we use the ``diversity'' metric \cite{ulmer2024}. Low diversity points to pathological problems (e.g., LLMs repeating previous comments). Conversely, extremely high diversity may indicate a lack of interaction between participants---a discussion where participants engage with each other will naturally feature some lexical overlap, including common terms or paraphrasing points made by others. We compare the distribution of diversity scores for synthetic discussions with that measured on sampled human discussions. This allows us to estimate the extent to which synthetic discussions approximate real-world content variety and participant interaction. 


\subsection{Technical Details}
\label{ssec:experimental:setup}

All experiments were collectively completed within five days of computational time, using two Quadro RTX 6000 GPUs. Toxicity evaluation was conducted using the Perspective API service. The execution script is available in the project's repository. We provide the $h=3$ last comments for context.

We examined five models for facilitation; two large (OLMo-32B, Qwen2.5-32B) and three smaller (LlaMa-8B, OLMo-7B and Qwen2.5-7B). While we experimented with smaller models, they could not handle the size of the input (context + instructions). For the participant agents, we used three smaller models (Gemma-4B and LlaMa-3B, Qwen2.5-4B), but dropped the latter due to degenerate output. All models are quantized to 4bits (\texttt{unsloth, bnb} variants).
