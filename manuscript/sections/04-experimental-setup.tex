\label{sec:experimental}

\subsection{Synthetic Discussion Generation}
\label{ssec:appendix:discussion}

We use a set of starting (``seed'') discussions taken from random points in escalated discussions featured in the CMV-Awry dataset \cite{chang-danescu-niculescu-mizil-2019-trouble} (see Appendix~\ref{TODO} for details). We then run $N_d=10$ discussions for each possible configuration of LLMs, turn-taking function, and facilitation strategy. Each discussion is composed of up to $30$ comments, including facilitator interventions.

We use different LLMs for participants (user-LLMs) and facilitators (mod-LLMs). While experimenting with different-sized LLMs we noticed that smaller LLMs (3-4B parameters) actually generated more varied, animated and direct speech, while larger models (8B-70B) respond in a more formulaic manner. Since the task of simulating a discussion is much easier compared to facilitating one, we decided to follow the paradigm proposed by~\cite{ulmer2024}, where a large model manages a scenario with specific goals and guidelines, while smaller models are used exclusively to interact with the former.
An overview of how the experiments are generated can be found in Alg.~\ref{alg:exp_generation}.



\begin{algorithm}[ht]
	\caption{Our \ac{sdg} implementation.
	\textit{Sample}: uniform sampling from a set.
	\textit{Actors}: instantiates an LLM agent given a model and a prompt.
	\textit{Post}: generates a comment given an actor, truncated discussion history, and (if moderator) a facilitation strategy.}

	\label{alg:exp_generation}
	
	\hspace*{\algorithmicindent} \textbf{Input:}
	\begin{itemize}[noitemsep,nosep]
		\item User SDBs $\Theta = \{\theta_{mod}, \theta_1,\dots,\theta_{45}\}$
		\item Strategies $S = \{s_1,s_2,s_3\}$
		\item Turn-taking functions $T=\{t_1,t_2,t_3\}$
		\item Seed discussions $S_d$
		\item Set of mod-LLMs $mod\_llms$, and user-LLMs $user\_llms$
		\item Number of experiments $N_e$, seed comments $N_{seed}$, discussion length $N_d$
	\end{itemize}
	
	\hspace*{\algorithmicindent} \textbf{Output:} Set of discussions $D$
	
	\begin{algorithmic}[1]
		\State $D \gets \emptyset$
		
		\For{$(user\_llm, mod\_llm, s, t) \in user\_llms \times mod\_llms \times S \times T$}
			\For{$exp = 1,2,\ldots,N_e$}
			
				\Statex \textit{\# Generate discussion configuration}
				\State $\hat{\Theta} \gets$ \Call{Sample}{$\Theta, 7$}
				\State $users \gets$ \Call{Actors}{$user\_llm, \hat{\Theta}$}
				\State $mod \gets$ \Call{Actors}{$mod\_llm, \theta_{mod}$}
				\State $seed \gets$ \Call{Sample}{$S_d, N_{seed}$}
				
				\Statex \textit{\# Initialize discussion with seed comments}
				\State $d \gets \emptyset$
				\For{$i = 1,2,\ldots,N_{seed}$}
					\State $(user, comment) \gets seed[i]$
					\State $d \gets d \cup (user, comment, i)$
				\EndFor
				
				\Statex \textit{\# Run discussion with LLM users}
				\State $last\_user \gets user$
				\For{$i = N_{seed}+1, N_{seed}+3, \ldots,N_{seed}+N_d$}
					\State $user \gets t(users, last\_user)$ \Comment{Pick next user}
					\State $last\_user \gets user$
					\State $comment \gets$ \Call{Post}{$user, d, i$} \Comment{User response}
					\State $d \gets d \cup (user, comment, i)$
					\State $comment \gets$ \Call{Post}{$mod, d, i+1, s$} \Comment{Optional facilitator intervention}
					\State $d \gets d \cup (user, comment, i+1)$
				\EndFor
				
				\State $D \gets D \cup \{d\}$
			\EndFor
		\EndFor
		
		\State \Return $D$
	\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}


\subsection{Facilitation Strategies}
\label{ssec:experimental:strategies}

We test two facilitation strategies used in real-life and compare them with two baselines. Note that the process of turning sometimes extensive documents into short prompts,necessitated by open-source LLMs,is necessarily imperfect. We leave the optimal derivation of strategy prompts to future work.

\begin{enumerate}[nosep,noitemsep]
    \item \textbf{\strategynomod}: A \emph{baseline} where no facilitator is present.

    \item \textbf{\strategynoinstr}: A \emph{baseline} where a LLM facilitator is present,but is provided only with basic instructions. This approach is already being used in some platforms \cite{Tsai_Deliberate_Lab_Open-Source_2025}. Example: “You are a moderator,keep the discussion civil”. 

    \item \textbf{\strategyregroom}: A \emph{real-life} strategy based on guidelines given to human facilitators of the ``Regulation Room'' platform \cite{Cornell_eRulemaking2017}. The instructions are suitable for online fora,where facilitators also engage in content moderation,and their effectiveness must be balanced by their throughput. Example: ``Stick to a maximum of two questions,use simple and clear language,deal with off-topic comments''.

    \item \textbf{\strategyconstrcomm}: A \emph{real-life} strategy based on the human facilitation guidelines \cite{dimitra-book}. It approaches facilitation from a more personalized and indirect angle,forbidding facilitators from directly providing opinions or directions. This makes the strategy ideal for deliberative environments. Example: ``Do not make decisions,be a guide,provide explanations''.
\end{enumerate}

In order to gauge the quality of our synthetic discussions,since we can not reliably measure ``realism'' (\S\ref{ssec:related:human-llm}),we use the ``diversity'' metric \cite{ulmer2024}. Low diversity points to pathological problems (e.g.,LLMs repeating previous comments). Conversely,extremely high diversity may indicate a lack of interaction between participants. A discussion where participants engage with each other will naturally feature some lexical overlap,including common terms or paraphrasing points made by others. We compare the distribution of diversity scores for synthetic discussions with that measured on sampled human discussions. This allows us to estimate the extent to which synthetic discussions approximate real-world content variety and participant interaction. 


\subsection{Technical Details}
\label{ssec:experimental:setup}

 We use four instruction-tuned,open-source models: LLaMa3.3 (70B),Qwen2.5 (33B),Mistral Nemo (12B) and OLMo2 (32B),quantized to 4 bits. All the experiments were collectively completed within four weeks of computational time,using two Quadro RTX 6000 GPUs. The execution script is available in the project's repository. We provide the $h=3$ last comments for context.
