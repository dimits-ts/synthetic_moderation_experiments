The modern social media environment has evolved to be extremely hostile, with users facing ever-increasing threats such as targeted misinformation \cite{clemons2025disinformation,Denniss2025Social}, hate speech \cite{kolluri2025parler}, and polarization \cite{pranesh2024impactsocialmediapolarization}. These threats can cause serious emotional and mental harm \cite{proactive_moderation}, radicalization \cite{cho-etal-2024-language}, real-world violence \cite{schaffner_community_guidelines}, sabotage democratic dialogue \cite{esau2017design,falk-etal-2021-predicting,seering_self_moderation}, and undermine trust in democratic institutions \cite{schroeder-etal-2024-fora}. These threats have been greatly amplified with the advent of Large Language Models (LLMs); malicious actors can use generative \ac{ai} technology to mass-produce misinformation~\cite{kreps2023ai, summerfield2024will}, attack online infrastructure~\cite{summerfield2024will} and overwhelm online communities with synthetic content~\cite{saura2025synthetification}. Platform designers and researchers have traditionally focused on identifying, flagging, and removing problematic content (called ``content moderation'' in literature)~\cite{seering_self_moderation,cresci_pesonalized_interventions}. However, these approaches are increasingly insufficient in practice~\cite{horta_automated_moderation, schaffner_community_guidelines, small-polis-llm, korre2025evaluation} and impose substantial strain on human moderators. Evidence shows that moderators experience elevated levels of psychological distress, including secondary trauma~\cite{spence2024content}, compassion fatigue, vicarious trauma, and burnout~\cite{spence2023psychological}, as well as exhaustion~\cite{steiger2021psychological}, with symptoms worsening through prolonged exposure~\cite{spence2024content}. Given the scale of threats to online communities and the growing capacity to mass-produce harmful content, reliance on a predominantly human-centered moderation model is increasingly untenable.

In light of the limitations of traditional content moderation, researchers have increasingly turned to approaches in which moderators actively engage with users through dialogue—often termed ``conversational moderation'' or ``facilitation''~\cite{argyle2023, korre2025evaluation, falk-etal-2021-predicting, cho-etal-2024-language}. Rather than reacting to violations after they occur, facilitation aims to preempt problematic behavior~\cite{cho-etal-2024-language,seering_self_moderation,cresci_pesonalized_interventions,make_reddit_great} while fostering deliberation and collective decision-making within communities~\cite{kim_et_al_chatbot,seering_self_moderation}. However, facilitators face many of the same psychological challenges as content moderators~\cite{schopke2024volunteer}, and cannot handle the sheer scale of modern-day social network traffic~\cite{schaffner_community_guidelines}, limiting the scalability of purely human-driven approaches.

Large Language Models (LLMs) have been proposed as scalable facilitators capable of supporting such interventions~\cite{korre2025evaluation, small-polis-llm}, yet experimentation and development remain costly and constrained by the need for human participation~\cite{rossi_2024, small-polis-llm}. Crucially, facilitation cannot be meaningfully evaluated in isolated, single-turn `listen-and-respond'' settings; it requires sustained conversational context~\cite{cho-etal-2024-language}, requiring multiple human participants to actively converse with the LLM facilitator. Since LLMs are pretrained on extensive human discussion data, researchers have hypothesized that they could replicate certain human behaviors in social science experiments~\cite{grossman_2023, tornberg_2023, argyle2023}---even though such behaviors must be validated against human data~\cite{adornetto2025_gabms, rossi_2024}. We therefore posit that fully simulated environments composed of LLM agents offer a rapid and cost-effective way of developing and ``debugging'' LLM-based facilitators---particularly in early stages, when system behavior may be unstable or unpredictable~\cite{atil_2025,rossi_2024}. While some studies have employed ad-hoc methodologies~\cite{mou_2024, park_simulacra, tornberg_2023, abdelnabi_negotiations, ulmer2024, park2024generativeagentsimulations1000}, \emph{there has been no study that addresses how to create, evaluate and use these simulations to solve real-world problems}. To address this gap, we introduce a new research direction ``\acf{sdg}'', a specific case of \acf{gabm}, which is a computational approach that simulates the interactions of agents to study complex behaviors and emergent phenomena. Unlike in generalized \ac{gabm} systems, \ac{sdg} actors do not have a predetermined set of actions and behaviors; rather, both are expressed through free text.

\begin{definition}
	\ac{sdg}: A task where the primary objective is to facilitate interactions among synthetic participants, and all such interactions are conducted exclusively through textual communication.
\end{definition}

To guide our investigation, we address two central research questions. Firstly:

\begin{research-question}
	How do we design and evaluate scalable\footnote{``Scalable'' in this context implies that the system can maintain consistent performance and quality as the scale of the experiments increase, without requiring disproportionate increases in computational resources or financial cost. } systems implementing \ac{sdg} simulations?
\end{research-question}

We begin by creating a theoretical, task-agnostic framework for \ac{sdg}. Since there are no standards or guidelines on how to create \ac{sdg} systems, we examine previous unsuccessful systems in the literature and use lessons from \acf{swe} to derive design and documentation rules (\S\ref{sec:methodology:design}). We break down the requirements for \ac{sdg} and derive a set of isolated components that must be implemented and separately evaluated (\S\ref{sec:methodology:initialization},~\S\ref{sec:methodology:management}). In order to determine whether observed behaviors are representative of real-world patterns, we build an evaluation suite grounded in \ac{gabm} literature and adapted for \ac{sdg}, which we use to verify the necessity and integrity of the above components (\S\ref{sec:methodology:evaluation}).

Having created a theoretical \ac{sdg} framework, we tackle the issue of scalability: what is the minimum required cost for running our experiments? 
Most recent work~\cite{abdelnabi_negotiations, park_simulacra, park2024generativeagentsimulations1000, balog_2024, tornberg_2023} uses proprietary models such as the OpenAI GPT family. We argue that the use of such models increases inference costs, which inherently limits the amount of experiments in academic settings, and the efficiency and scalability of such systems on real-world problems (\S\ref{sec:scalability:cost}). Indeed, we show that using small (7B-8B parameter) quantized models is enough for many \ac{sdg} tasks (\S\ref{sec:scalability:model}). By developing a methodology for cost-comparison between experiments with humans, cloud-based, and local LLMs we estimate that our methodology using smaller models achieves a \emph{thousand-time decrease} in costs compared to experiments with humans, and a \emph{twenty-time decrease} compared to the recent, middle-capability GPT-5.1 model (Table~\ref{tab:cost}), while still observing emergent social phenomena in our experiments (Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix:behaviors}).  

Having a completed \ac{sdg} framework we can leverage the generated simulations to run experiments for LLM facilitation in online discussions by tackling the second research question:

\begin{research-question}
	Can we derive useful, actionable insights for a social science task using engineered \ac{sdg} simulations?
\end{research-question}

While the use of LLM participants and the relatively limited recent work on facilitation fundamentally constrain the extent of possible findings, our experiments uncover generalizable results. Specifically, we discover a severe limitation not yet discovered in the literature: LLM facilitators cannot choose \emph{not to} intervene, even when interventions are unnecessary (\S\ref{sec:facilitation:interventions}). This behavior has been observed to lead to frustration and toxicity among human participants in online communities~\cite{korre2025evaluation}---a behavior that is replicated in our experiments by the LLM participants (Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix:behaviors}), among several other social phenomena observed in social science literature. We also confirm that LLM facilitators are able to alter conversational dynamics according to different facilitation strategies (\S\ref{sec:facilitation:strategies}).

Finally, we release an open-source Python framework that implements our methodology at scale (\S\ref{sec:materials:software}), enabling the research community to rapidly experiment with synthetically generated discussions. Given that existing facilitation datasets are few and generally small \cite{korre2025evaluation}, we also release a large, publicly available dataset with LLM-generated and annotated synthetic discussions (\S\ref{sec:materials:data}). Our dataset can be used for LLM facilitator fine-tuning \cite{ulmer2024} as well as for analyzing the behavior of out-of-the-box LLMs in the task of online facilitation. We use open-source LLMs and include all relevant configurations in order to make our study as reproducible as possible.
