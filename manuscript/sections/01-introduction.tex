\label{sec:introduction}

The modern social media environment has evolved to be extremely demanding, with users facing ever-increasing threats such as targeted misinformation \cite{clemons2025disinformation,Denniss2025Social}, hate speech \cite{kolluri2025parler}, and polarization \cite{pranesh2024impactsocialmediapolarization}. 
These threats can cause serious emotional and mental harm \cite{proactive_moderation}, radicalization \cite{cho-etal-2024-language}, real-world violence \cite{schaffner_community_guidelines}, sabotage democratic dialogue \cite{esau2017design,falk-etal-2021-predicting,seering_self_moderation}, and undermine trust in democratic institutions \cite{schroeder-etal-2024-fora}. Platform designers and researchers traditionally focused on flagging and removing problematic content (``content moderation''---\cite{seering_self_moderation,cresci_pesonalized_interventions}), but these methods are no longer sufficient in practice \cite{horta_automated_moderation,schaffner_community_guidelines,small-polis-llm,korre2025evaluation}. However, online communities benefit when moderators actively discuss with users.; an approach known as ``conversational moderation'' or ``facilitation'' \cite{argyle2023,korre2025evaluation,falk-etal-2021-predicting}). Facilitation prevents problematic user behavior before it surfaces \cite{cho-etal-2024-language,seering_self_moderation,cresci_pesonalized_interventions,make_reddit_great}, and supports community deliberation and group decision-making \cite{kim_et_al_chatbot,seering_self_moderation}. Large Language Models (LLMs) have been hypothesized to be capable of facilitation tasks and can be scaled to a far greater extent compared to human facilitators \cite{korre2025evaluation,small-polis-llm}, making them a viable choice for modern large-scale social networks. However, experimentation and development on these systems is hampered due to the costs of human participation~\cite{rossi_2024}. 

We posit that simulations with all-LLM-agents can be a cheap and fast way to develop and test LLM facilitators, initial versions of which may be unstable or unpredictable \cite{atil_2025,rossi_2024}, before testing them with human participants. We investigate a new research direction, ``\acf{sdg}'', which is defined as:

\begin{definition}
	\acf{sdg}: A task where the primary objective is to facilitate interactions among synthetic participants, and all interactions are conducted exclusively through textual communication.
\end{definition}

From this definition, it becomes clear that \ac{sdg} is a specific case of \acf{gabm}, a computational approach that simulates the interactions of autonomous agents to generate and study complex behaviors and emergent phenomena in systems. Unlike generalized \ac{gabm} systems, \ac{sdg} actors do not have a predetermined set of actions and behaviors; rather both are expressed by free text. 

Since there are no standards or guidelines on how to create \ac{sdg} systems, we examine previous unsuccessful systems in literature and use lessons from \ac{cs} in order to derive design rules for these systems (\S\ref{sec:methodology:design:rules}). We also propose a new documentation system, ``\ac{ocaso}'', which is used to effectively communicate the goals, requirements, and implementation of each component in our system (\S\ref{sec:methodology:design:doc}). These components belong in two major categories; discussion initialization (personas, roles, instructions and seed opinions) which seeks to create varied discussions, and discussion management (context management, turn taking) which define the fundamental mechanics required for the execution of synthetic discussions (\S\ref{sec:methodology:initialization}). Finally, we build an evaluation suite, grounded in \ac{gabm} literature and adapted for \ac{sdg}, which we use to verify the necessity and integrity of our components (\S\ref{sec:methodology:evaluation}).

Having created a theoretical \ac{sdg} framework we tackle the issue of scalability; what is minimum required cost for running our experiments? We begin by examining four pairs of open-source LLMs each of which is composed of smaller and larger variants of same-family, same-version models used in research (\S\ref{sec:scalability:model}). We find that there is not a single model that fits all requirements for our simulations---in our case Qwen-7B excels in producing human-like content variety, while LLaMa-8B creates escalating conversational dynamics---and that generally, small, quantized LLMs are capable for most \ac{sdg} requirements. Furthermore, we investigate different deployment setups used in literature(how LLM participants are mapped to the underlying models). While there are many methodologically valid setups, we opt for a single local model setup in order to lower infrastructure requirements (\S\ref{sec:scalability:setup}). In order to compare the viability of \ac{sdg} we analyze the costs and constraints imposed by locally hosted, open-source LLMs, proprietary, cloud-based LLMs and human participants (\S\ref{sec:scalability:cost}). We identify different sets of challenges depending on the nature of monetary costs---\acf{opex} tend to constrain the ``width'' of experiments (how many experiments we can run), while \acf{capex} constraints experimental ``depth'' (how capable our simulations are). By adapting an existing proposed methodology for cost-comparison, we calculate that our setup, when using smaller LLMs, is orders of magnitude cheaper than using human participants, and proprietary models dominating current social-science research (\S\ref{sec:scalability:open-source}).

We then use our \ac{sdg} implementation to run experiments for LLM facilitation in online discussions. Our results uncover a severe limitation not yet discovered in literature; LLM facilitators can not generally choose not to intervene, even when interventions are unnecessary (\S\ref{sec:facilitation:interventions}). This behavior has been observed to lead to frustration and toxicity among human participants in online communities~\cite{korre2025evaluation}---a behavior which is replicated in our experiments by the LLM participants (\S\ref{sec:facilitation:behaviors}) among several other social phenomena observed in social science literature. We also confirm that LLM facilitators are able to alter conversational dynamics according to different facilitation strategies (\S\ref{sec:facilitation:strategies}).

Finally, we release an open-source Python framework that implements our methodology at scale (\S\ref{sec:discussion:software}), enabling the research community to rapidly experiment with synthetically generated discussions. Given that existing facilitation datasets are few and generally small~\cite{korre2025evaluation}, we also release a large, publicly available dataset with LLM-generated and annotated synthetic discussions (\S\ref{sec:discussion:data}). Our dataset can be used for LLM facilitator finetuning~\cite{ulmer2024}, as well as for analyzing the behavior of out-of-the-box LLMs in the task of online facilitation. We use open-source LLMs and include all relevant configurations in order to make our study as reproducible as possible.
