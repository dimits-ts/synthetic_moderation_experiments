\section{Replication Notes}
\label{sec:appendix:replication}

\subsection{Experiments}
\label{sec:appendix:replication:experiments}

The latest version of our framework (\texttt{2.0.7} at the time of writing) was the one used in our experiments. Researchers replicating this work should downgrade to this version. 

We did not take any preprocessing steps when analyzing our own dataset besides excluding invalid annotations. This was necessary since the model did not always respond with a valid annotation score (we note that this refusal was rare). The only preprocessing performed on the \ac{ceri} Regulation Room dataset is the removal of HTML tags from the comment text. 

For the seed discussions we use the CMV-Awry dataset \cite{chang-danescu-niculescu-mizil-2019-trouble}, as stated in the main text. We remove comments from deleted users. In order to select ``escalated'' discussions, we use the \texttt{derailment} values provided by the dataset. Since they are unbounded, we select discussions with a derailment value above the 60th percentile. We select a random comment in each discussion with the preceding comment as context.

Finally, it's worth noting that while we have made efforts to make the study as reproducible as possible by releasing the code, inputs, using open-source models and running the experiments under set seeds, we can not guarantee exact replication due to the inherent randomness in LLMs which can only be mitigated \cite{atil_2025,bisbee_2023}. Likewise, the instruction prompts as well as other hyper-parameters may have been implicitly optimized during the many iterations of this study.


\subsection{Analysis}
\label{sec:appendix:replication:analysis}

Our Jensen--Shannon divergence estimates rely on several methodological assumptions. First, we approximate the underlying diversity distributions using Gaussian kernel density estimation (KDE) evaluated on a fixed grid over $[0,1]$, implicitly assuming smooth, continuous distributions and sufficient sample sizes for stable density estimation. In our experiments, the ablation conditions have fewer discussions than the main dataset, which is necessary even with relatively low inference costs (\S\ref{sec:scalability:cost}) due to the multidimensional exploratory nature of our approach. We assume that the density estimation is accurate enough with at least $n=30$ discussions. Second, the choice of bandwidth (as determined by the default \texttt{gaussian\_kde} implementation) and the number of evaluation bins (fixed to 50) can affect the resulting divergence scores; alternative bandwidth selection strategies or grid resolutions may yield quantitatively different results. We also remove empty comments from the calculations.


\subsection{Seed dataset preprocessing}
\label{sec:appendix:replication:preprocessing}

As a preprocessing step, we remove comments that contain no textual content and exclude discussions involving fewer than two distinct participants, ensuring that all retained threads constitute meaningful interactions. Our data are drawn from the PEFK dataset~\cite{tsirmpas2025pefk}. To operationalize escalation, we rely on the derailment score provided in the official release. Because these scores are model-derived and not directly interpretable on an absolute scale, we adopt a percentile-based threshold: a discussion is labeled as \emph{escalated} if its derailment value falls within the top 60th percentile of the distribution. This relative criterion ensures comparability while avoiding assumptions about the intrinsic scale of the original model outputs.


\section{Cost Calculation}
\label{sec:appendix:cost}

\subsection{Methodology}

\paragraph{Open-source models}
For self-hosted open-source LLMs, we estimate the \acf{cpt} by explicitly modeling the  \ac{tco} over the actual duration of the experiment, rather than over a full annual cycle. This provides a conservative and deployment-aware estimate for research settings where infrastructure is utilized only for a limited time window. 

We decompose the \ac{tco} for the experiment ($\text{TCO}_{\text{exp}}$) into \ac{capex} and \ac{opex}, where \ac{opex} includes electricity costs:

\[
\text{TCO}_{\text{exp}} = \text{CapEx}_{\text{exp}} + \text{PowerCost}_{\text{exp}}.
\]

\ac{capex} is computed by amortizing the initial server investment over a fixed depreciation period (typically 4 years), and scaling it proportionally to the experiment duration:

\[
\text{CapEx}_{\text{exp}} =
C_{\text{server}} \cdot N_{\text{servers}}
\cdot
\frac{D_{\text{exp}}}{365 \cdot Y_{\text{depr}}},
\]

\noindent where:
\begin{itemize}
	\item $C_{\text{server}}$ is the initial cost per server,
	\item $N_{\text{servers}}$ is the number of servers used,
	\item $Y_{\text{depr}}$ is the depreciation period in years,
	\item $D_{\text{exp}}$ is the experiment duration in days.
\end{itemize}

Operational expenditure includes electricity consumption over the experiment window. Note that this formulation does not account for other operational costs such as software licenses (since most tools used in \ac{nlp} are open-source), or personnel (since it is unlikely that dedicated staff members maintain the servers in research settings).

\[
\text{PowerCost}_{\text{exp}} =
\frac{P_{\text{watts}}}{1000}
\cdot (24 \cdot D_{\text{exp}})
\cdot C_{\text{kWh}}
\cdot N_{\text{servers}},
\]

\noindent where:
\begin{itemize}
	\item $P_{\text{watts}}$ is the average power draw per server (in watts),
	\item $C_{\text{kWh}}$ is the electricity price per kilowatt-hour.
\end{itemize}

To convert $\text{TCO}_{\text{exp}}$ into a cost per task, we estimate the total number of tasks completed during the experiment using system throughput. Let $R_{\text{ps}}$ denote requests per second per instance, $N_{\text{inst}}$ the number of model instances, $U$ the uptime fraction, and $R_{\text{task}}$ the number of model requests required per task. The total number of tasks completed during the experiment is:

\[
\text{Tasks}_{\text{exp}} =
\frac{
	R_{\text{ps}} \cdot N_{\text{inst}} \cdot U
	\cdot (24 \cdot 3600 \cdot D_{\text{exp}})
}{
	R_{\text{task}}
}.
\]

Finally, the Cost Per Task for self-hosted open-source models is:

\[
\text{CPT}_{\text{Open-Source}} =
\frac{\text{TCO}_{\text{exp}}}{\text{Tasks}_{\text{exp}}}.
\]

This formulation makes explicit that cost efficiency depends critically on hardware utilization (requests per second and uptime) and on the number of model calls required per task. Underutilized infrastructure or excessive request counts per discussion significantly increase the effective CPT, while higher throughput and stable uptime reduce it.


\paragraph{Proprietary models}
The task of calculating \ac{cpt} for proprietary models is significantly easier, since their service has a clear pricing structure. The \ac{cpt} for Proprietary LLMs ($CPT_{\text{Proprietary}}$) is calculated by separating the expense for the Input Sequence Length (ISL) and the Output Sequence Length (OSL), using the respective prices ($P_{\text{in}}$ and $P_{\text{out}}$) per million tokens. Unlike \cite{pan2025_cost_benefit}, we do not explicitly consider model throughput in our calculations, since there is no monetary penalty for slower generation---the cost is only dependent on the input and output tokens. The \ac{cpt} for proprietary models is thus defined as:

\[
\text{CPT}_{\text{Proprietary}} = \frac{(\text{ISL} \times P_{\text{in}} + \text{OSL} \times P_{\text{out}})}{1,000,000} + C_{\text{API Overhead}} 
\]
\noindent where $C_{\text{API Overhead}}$ includes costs from failed calls and vendor usage fees. For multi-turn discussions, ISL and OSL denote the \textit{non-cumulative} input and output token counts across all model calls required to complete the task.


\paragraph{Human participation}

Human participation costs can not, and should not, be calculated in a vacuum. There are certainly ways to get participates with wages much lower than minimum wage for most countries, and there are even platforms such as Mechanical Turk which streamline this process. Besides the obvious and severe ethical implications of doing so, this often results in poor quality data \cite{Moss2023}. The true cost of procuring human labor in a research context is calculated using the (ethical) gross hourly wage, mandatory platform fees, and quality assurance overhead:

The human labor rate is converted to $CPT_{\text{Human}}$ by estimating the time required to complete the specific research task:
\[
CPT_{\text{Human}} =(\text{Time Per Task}_{\text{est}} \times W_{\text{gross}}) \times(1 + F_{\text{platform}}) + C_{\text{QA per Task}}
\]
\noindent where:
\begin{itemize}
	\item $W_{\text{gross}}$ is the ethical gross hourly wage (e.g., minimum recommended rate of \$8.00–\$12.00/hour).
	\item $F_{\text{platform}}$ is the commission charged by crowdsourcing platforms (e.g., $33.3\%$ for academic use on Prolific or $20-40\%$ on MTurk).
	\item $C_{\text{QA-overhead}}$ is the amortized internal labor cost incurred by the research team for quality control, screening, and dropout management.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Assumptions}

We assume that no data are thrown out for data quality reasons in any of the tasks. Our own experimental setup (\S\ref{sec:scalability:experimental}) uses only two GPUs (retail cost 3000\$ /GPU), therefore, we assume an initial server cost of 7000\$ for CAPEX. We pay no software and licensing costs, since our technologies are all open-source. Since the server utilization is extremely short (5 days) we do not account for operational costs other than electricity and GPU depreciation. The budget setup assumes a consumer-grade computer with on-board GPU which costs no additional expenses to the researchers, which draws half the power of a GPU server.

We compare our setup with an OpenAI GPT-5.2 instance for proprietary models. The GPT-5 pricing model as of the time of writing (Q1 2026), is 1.77\$ per million input tokens and 14\$ per million output tokens. The costs can be adjusted depending on the model and future pricing changes. We selected this specific model as a good compromise between model capability and cost considerations---cheaper models are explicitly marketed as competent in ``well-defined tasks''~\cite{openai-pricing}, which ours is not.

For the human participation comparison we assume a task using Prolific as a platform, which currently charges a 33\% overhead, and a fair wage of 12\$ per hour, per participant. In our experiments, we used 7 participants per discussion, hence the total number of tasks changes proportionally. We also assume that each discussion takes five real-world minutes to complete.


\subsection{Experiment cost estimation}

Each discussion is treated as a standalone task in our calculations. Our dataset contains a total of $2{,}273$ discussions, each consisting of $20$ comments.\footnote{Except for the experiments without a moderator. Since these experiments are comparatively few, we omit them from this calculation for simplicity.} The mean comment length is $294$ words, which corresponds to approximately $393$ tokens using the $0.75$ tokens/word rule. Assuming an instruction prompt of $300$ words ($\approx 400$ tokens), the LLM produces roughly $393$ output tokens per comment and receives $393 \times 3 + 400 = 1{,}579$ input tokens. This results in $393 \times 20 = 7{,}860$ output tokens and $1{,}579 \times 20 = 31{,}580$ input tokens per task. This analysis assumes that proprietary and open-source models generate approximately the same number of tokens for this task.

\subsection{Participant Instructions}
\label{sssec:appendix:roles}

Below are the instructions for normal users and troll users respectively. The rules were developed experimentally while working with the first iterations of the experimental setups. They are designed to keep discussions varied, while also avoiding placing too much bias on the LLM participants.


\subsubsection{Normal users}

\paragraph{Instructions}
\begin{itemize}
	\item Write short, natural comments similar to those of typical forum users.
	\item Refer to others using @username.
	\item You may express strong opinions and frustration.
	\item You may react more strongly if you feel repeatedly provoked.
	\item You know that a moderator is present and can ban you.
	\item If you want to continue participating, avoid behavior that would obviously lead to a ban.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Output Format}
\begin{itemize}
	\item Provide only your forum comment.
	\item If staying silent, output the empty string "".
\end{itemize}


\subsubsection{Troll users}

\paragraph{Instructions}
\begin{itemize}
	\item Write short, natural comments similar to those of typical forum users.
	\item Refer to others using @username.
	\item Post comments intended to annoy, frustrate, or provoke other users.
	\item Use sarcasm, exaggeration, or playful mockery.
	\item You may challenge other users’ opinions and push their buttons.
	\item Maintain the illusion of being a normal user while trolling.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Output Format}
\begin{itemize}
	\item Provide only your forum comment.
	\item If staying silent, output the empty string """"".
\end{itemize}


\subsubsection{No Instructions}
\paragraph{Output Format}
\begin{itemize}
	\item Provide only your forum comment.
	\item If staying silent, output the empty string """"".
\end{itemize}


\subsection{Facilitation Strategies}
\label{ssec:appendix:moderation_strategies}


\subsubsection{Constructive communications}

\paragraph{General Rules}
\begin{itemize}
	\item Respond only if absolutely necessary. Otherwise, remain silent.
	\item You are a human moderator in an online forum discussion.
	\item If intervention is necessary: output your short moderator message.
	\item If not: output ``''.
	\item When responding, write concise, natural-sounding messages. Refer to users as @username.
	\item You may issue warnings. You may take disciplinary action (e.g., banning a user)
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{The Role of the Moderator}
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Environment and Care:} Moderators create a warm environment, treating participants as human beings first.
	\item \textbf{Process Management:} Duties include timekeeping, monitoring energy levels, and managing physical needs like bio breaks or stretching.
	\item \textbf{Participation Balancing:} Mods must encourage shared ``air time,'', normalize silence for thinking, and ensure participants talk to each other rather than through the moderator.
	\item \textbf{Conflict and Behavior:} The role involves highlighting points of disagreement to reach understanding and interrupting problematic behaviors like microaggressions or personal slights.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Limitations and Boundaries (``What NOT to do'')}
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Non-Directive Approach:} Moderators are not decision-makers; they act as ``support staff'' or ``tour managers'' rather than conductors.
	\item \textbf{Deferring to participants:} Legitimacy lies with the participants, who set their own agenda and write/edit all content.
	\item \textbf{Content Neutrality:} Mods must not use their own knowledge of sustainability or fact-check participants, maintaining a strict separation between process and content.
	\item \textbf{Minimal Intervention:} Intervention is only permitted for reasons of respect, safety, or process clarification.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Tools for Effective Facilitation}
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Active Listening:} Techniques include nodding, summarizing, and focusing on experiences rather than opinions.
	\item \textbf{Non-Content Prompts:} Helpful phrases include ``Tell me more,'' ``What is the story behind that?'', and ``Who hasn’t had a chance to weigh in?''
	\item \textbf{Bias Awareness:} Moderators are encouraged to identify subconscious biases toward activists or administrators and to intentionally engage with ideas they may instinctively dislike.
\end{itemize}


\subsubsection{Constructive communications}

\paragraph{General Rules}
\begin{itemize}
	\item Respond only if absolutely necessary. Otherwise, remain silent.
	\item You are a human moderator in an online forum discussion.
	\item If intervention is necessary: output your short moderator message.
	\item If not: output "".
	\item When responding, write concise, natural-sounding messages. Refer to users as @username.
	\item You may issue warnings. You may take disciplinary action (e.g., banning a user)
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Foundational Principles}
\begin{itemize}
	\item Maintain Neutrality: Remain neutral at all times. Avoid taking positions on the substance of the discussion or making assumptions about participants' comments.
	\item Practice Active Listening: Carefully read and reflect on what the commenter is saying before thinking of a response.
	\item Model Desired Behavior: Maintain a positive tone and show respect to all participants to encourage a "knowledge building community".
	\item Use Plain Language: Use short sentences and common, everyday words that the audience can understand the first time they read them.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Engagement \& Tone}
\begin{itemize}
	\item Be Welcoming and Unique: Make participants feel appreciated and part of the community. Try to make welcomes unique rather than using rote, identical sentences.
	\item Limit Questions: To avoid confusing or intimidating the commenter, limit responses to one or two questions.
	\item Show Intellectual Curiosity: Model a spirit of inquiry and a desire to learn from the commenter’s experience and views.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Facilitating Effective Comments}
\begin{itemize}
	\item Mentor Effective Commenting: Move participants past “voting and venting” behaviors toward contributing substantive information they possess.
	\item Request Substantiation: Encourage commenters to provide personal experiences, factual details, or data to support their claims.
	\item Provide Clarification: If a comment is unclear, paraphrase what you think they are saying and ask for confirmation.
	\item Redirect and Refocus: If a comment is off-topic or in the wrong place, direct the user to the correct issue post or clarify what the agency is looking for.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Community Supervision \& Technical Support}
\begin{itemize}
	\item Police Civility: Intervene when interactions become heated, offensive, or involve personalized attacks.
\end{itemize}
